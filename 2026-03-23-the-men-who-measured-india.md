# The Men Who Measured India — With Triangles, Fever, and Stubbornness

*March 23, 2026 — Technology / History*

On the morning of April 10, 1802, a middle-aged British infantry officer named William Lambton stood on a flat plain south of Madras and squinted through a half-ton theodolite at a hilltop 7.5 miles away. He was about to draw the first line in what would become the most ambitious measurement project in human history — a project that would take not the five years the East India Company budgeted for, but nearly seventy. A project that would kill hundreds of men, break bodies and minds, and ultimately produce something extraordinary: the precise shape of India, and the discovery of the tallest mountain on Earth.

This is the story of the Great Trigonometrical Survey.

## The Madman's Geometry

The idea was deceptively simple. If you know the exact distance between two points — a "baseline" — and you can see a third point from both of them, then by measuring the angles of the triangle formed, you can calculate the distance to that third point without ever walking there. That's triangulation. A technique as old as ancient Greece.

Lambton's audacious plan was to chain these triangles together, one after another, marching a line of triangulation straight up the spine of India from Cape Comorin at the southern tip to the Himalayas in the north — a distance of roughly 1,600 miles. This "Great Arc of the Meridian" would not just map India. It would help answer one of the burning scientific questions of the age: what is the exact shape of the Earth?

The Earth isn't a perfect sphere. Newton had predicted it was slightly flattened at the poles, bulging at the equator. But by how much? The only way to know was to measure a long arc of meridian — a north-south line — with excruciating precision and compare it to astronomical observations. India, stretching from 8° to 37° north latitude, offered the longest possible canvas.

## Seven Hundred People and a Half-Ton Instrument

The Great Theodolite, built by William Cary of London, weighed over 1,000 pounds. Its circular brass plate was 36 inches across. Moving it required twelve men and a custom carriage. To get clean sight lines between triangulation points, the survey team often had to build towers, scaffolding, and platforms on hilltops, temples, and forts — sometimes seventy or eighty feet high — then haul the theodolite to the top.

At its peak, the survey party numbered 700 people: soldiers for protection, laborers to carry equipment and build towers, chain-bearers to measure baselines with steel chains (later replaced by ten-foot iron bars for better accuracy), axemen to clear lines of sight through jungle, and a handful of trained surveyors and mathematicians to do the actual measuring and calculating.

Every observation had to be taken multiple times and averaged. Every angle was measured to a fraction of an arc-second. The baseline near Madras — those first 7.5 miles — took 57 days to measure, with Lambton personally supervising every link of the chain.

## The Jungle Eats Men

The precision was extraordinary. The human cost was staggering.

Lambton drove his survey south through the jungles of the Deccan Plateau and then turned north for the Great Arc. The terrain was merciless. Dense tropical forest. Malarial swamps. Tigers. Cobras. Spiders the size of birds. Monsoon rains that turned everything to mud and made observations impossible for months at a time.

Malaria was the great killer. It didn't just pick off individuals — it took out entire survey parties. When George Everest, Lambton's young chief assistant, led a team into the jungle between the Godavari and Krishna rivers during monsoon season, every single person in his 150-man party contracted malaria. They had to abandon the survey and stagger back to Hyderabad.

Everest himself was devastated by the disease. His limbs became paralyzed. His skin peeled. He suffered nightmares and delirium. He was eventually shipped to the Cape of Good Hope to recover — a journey of weeks — and it took him months before he could stand again.

He came back and kept surveying.

Lambton, for his part, burned the retina of his left eye by observing a solar eclipse through the theodolite without darkened glasses. He spent most of his personal earnings supporting his aging parents back in England and lived in near-poverty in the field. In January 1823, near Hinganghat in present-day Maharashtra, fever finally killed him. He was approximately 70 years old. He had been measuring India for twenty-one years. The Arc had reached only halfway.

## Enter Everest

George Everest took over and, if anything, was even more obsessive than Lambton. He was a perfectionist of almost pathological intensity — a man who feuded with colleagues, ignored his superiors, redesigned instruments to shave fractions of error, and drove his teams to the edge of endurance.

He replaced Lambton's steel survey chains with precisely calibrated iron bars, eliminating the small errors caused by chains sagging under their own weight. He redesigned the theodolite to be lighter and more portable. He developed new methods for compensating for atmospheric refraction — the bending of light in hot air that could throw off observations by crucial amounts.

And he pushed the Arc north, through the fever-ridden plains of central India, through the tangled forests of what is now Madhya Pradesh, through territory where the survey had to negotiate passage with local rulers and occasionally dodge armed bandits. By 1843, despite repeated collapses of his health, Everest had extended the Great Arc to Banog in the Himalayan foothills near Mussoorie — a total length of 2,400 kilometers.

He retired to England that year, half-broken by decades of tropical disease, and was knighted for his efforts.

## The Mathematician from Calcutta

But the story's most remarkable chapter was still being written — in an office in Calcutta, not on a mountaintop.

After Everest's retirement, Andrew Scott Waugh took over as Surveyor General. The survey had begun observing the Himalayan peaks from the plains — you can see mountains from enormous distances if you're measuring angles precisely enough. A peak designated "Peak XV" had been observed from six different stations, more than 100 miles away, but its height calculations were a mess. Atmospheric refraction, the curvature of the Earth, and the sheer distance involved made the mathematics fiendishly complex.

The man tasked with untangling this data was Radhanath Sikdar, a Bengali mathematician who had joined the survey in 1831 at a salary of thirty rupees a month. Sikdar was the survey's chief "computer" — a job title that in the 1850s meant a human being who computed. He was, by all accounts, brilliant. He had studied under the great mathematician Tithunath Ghosh at Hindu College in Calcutta, and he brought a rigorous command of spherical trigonometry and the method of least squares to the survey's mountain of raw data.

Working through the observations of Peak XV, applying corrections for refraction, temperature, barometric pressure, and the Earth's curvature, Sikdar arrived at a height: exactly 29,000 feet.

Waugh, worried that such a perfectly round number would look like a guess rather than a measurement, added two arbitrary feet. When the result was officially announced in March 1856, the height of Peak XV was declared to be 29,002 feet. (The modern accepted height, re-measured by India in 1955 and confirmed by China and Nepal since, is 29,031.7 feet — Sikdar's original calculation was off by a mere 0.1%.)

The mountain was named after George Everest, despite his objections — he pointed out, correctly, that "Everest" could not be written in Hindi or pronounced by the local population. He never saw the peak that bears his name.

## Seventy Years of Triangles

The Great Trigonometrical Survey was officially completed in 1871, though related survey work continued for decades afterward. In those seventy years, survey teams had measured the entire subcontinent in a grid of triangulation chains running north-south and east-west. They had determined the heights of the world's tallest mountains. They had produced one of the first precise measurements of a meridian arc, contributing directly to our understanding of the Earth's shape. They had discovered geodesic anomalies — places where the gravitational pull of the Himalayas literally bent their plumb lines sideways — that led to the theory of isostasy, the idea that mountains have deep "roots" of lighter rock floating on denser material below.

The cumulative death toll — from malaria, cholera, tiger attacks, falls from survey towers, heatstroke, and simple exhaustion — was enormous, though never precisely counted. Historian John Keay estimated that the survey's casualties exceeded those of many contemporary military campaigns. Most of the dead were the unnamed Indian laborers, chain-bearers, and porters who did the brutal physical work of hauling equipment through impossible terrain.

## Why It Matters

We live in an age where your phone can tell you your position on Earth to within a few feet, instantly, for free. It's worth pausing to consider that this capability rests on a foundation laid by people who spent decades in malarial jungles, squinting through brass instruments, doing arithmetic by hand, and dying in enormous numbers — all to answer the question: *where, exactly, are we?*

The Great Trigonometrical Survey didn't just map India. It helped map the Earth itself. It proved that pure mathematics, applied with sufficient stubbornness and at sufficient human cost, could measure a planet. And it revealed, almost as an afterthought, the highest point on that planet's surface — a peak so tall that a mathematician in Calcutta could calculate its height from observations taken a hundred miles away, and get it right to within thirty feet.

That's not just surveying. That's one of the great intellectual achievements of the nineteenth century. And almost nobody has heard of it.
