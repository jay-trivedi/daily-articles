# The Night Witches Who Flew on Broomsticks

*March 21, 2026 — History*

In the summer of 1942, German soldiers stationed along the Eastern Front began reporting something strange. At night, with no warning, bombs would fall from the sky. There was no engine roar. No searchlight could find the attackers in time. The only sound — and this is the part that crawled under their skin — was a soft whooshing, like a broomstick sweeping through the air. Then an explosion. Then silence.

The soldiers started calling them *die Nachthexen*. The Night Witches.

They weren't witches, of course. They were women — most of them teenagers and twenty-somethings — flying plywood biplanes held together with canvas and wire, dropping bombs on the Wehrmacht night after night for three years straight. And they were terrifyingly good at it.

## A Phone Call to Stalin

The whole thing started because of one woman: Marina Raskova. Before the war, Raskova was the most famous female pilot in the Soviet Union — the "Russian Amelia Earhart." In 1938, she'd set a world record for non-stop distance flight by a woman, crash-landing in the Siberian taiga after running out of fuel and surviving ten days in the wilderness with nothing but a pistol and a chocolate bar.

When Germany invaded the Soviet Union in June 1941, thousands of women volunteered for combat duty. Most were turned away. But Raskova had something the others didn't — she had Stalin's personal phone number. She called, she convinced, and on October 8, 1941, Stalin ordered the formation of three all-female aviation units. One of them, the 588th Night Bomber Aviation Regiment, would become the Night Witches.

The regiment was placed under the command of Major Yevdokiya Bershanskaya — the only woman to command a Soviet bomber unit during the entire war. Her pilots were almost all volunteers between the ages of seventeen and twenty-six. Many had been students, factory workers, schoolteachers. They were given six months to learn what male aviators trained for years to do.

## The Worst Plane in the War

Here's where the story gets absurd. The aircraft they were assigned wasn't a bomber. It was a Polikarpov Po-2 — a biplane designed in 1928 for crop dusting and flight training. The Soviets called it the *Kukuruznik*, which roughly translates to "crop-duster." It was made of plywood and stretched canvas. It had an open cockpit. Its top speed was about 150 km/h — slower than the stall speed of a German Messerschmitt, meaning a German fighter literally could not fly slowly enough to engage one without falling out of the sky.

The Po-2 could carry exactly two bombs. Two. Each sortie — fly to the target, drop both bombs, fly back — took about 30 to 50 minutes. So to do any meaningful damage, the Night Witches flew eight, ten, sometimes *eighteen* bombing runs in a single night. Each run over enemy territory. Each run through anti-aircraft fire.

And here's the detail that will stick with you: they didn't carry parachutes. Not because of some death-wish bravado, but because the bombs were so heavy relative to the tiny aircraft that the weight of a parachute would have meant carrying one fewer bomb. They flew without parachutes until 1944. For two years, if your plywood plane caught fire — and canvas burns very easily — there was no getting out.

## The Whooshing of Broomsticks

Their signature technique was ingenious in its simplicity. As they approached a target, the lead pilot would fly in with the engine running, drawing searchlight fire. While the Germans swung their lights toward the decoy, a second and third plane would cut their engines entirely and *glide* in — silent, invisible, carrying nothing but wind noise and two bombs.

The Germans said the sound of the gliding planes reminded them of broomsticks sweeping the sky. Hence: Night Witches. The name was meant to be derogatory. The women wore it like a badge of honor.

The psychological effect was devastating. German soldiers reportedly stopped lighting cigarettes at night — the glow of a match could draw a bomb. They lay awake listening for that soft whoosh in the darkness. Unable to explain how these primitive planes kept evading their defenses, some Germans theorized the women had been given special injections that let them see in the dark, or that they were criminals being punished with suicide missions.

The Wehrmacht was so frustrated that they offered a guaranteed Iron Cross — one of Germany's highest military decorations — to any soldier who managed to shoot down a single Night Witch.

## Forty-Two Bullet Holes

The Night Witches paid for every sortie. Thirty-two pilots were killed during the war. The losses were felt deeply in a unit this tight-knit — these women lived together, flew together, and mourned together in the frozen mud of makeshift airfields.

But the stories of survival are just as staggering. Nadezhda Popova, who would become one of the regiment's most celebrated pilots, once returned from a single sortie with 42 bullet holes in her aircraft. Forty-two. Holes in the fuselage, holes in the wings, holes in her map, a hole in her helmet. She landed the shot-up crop duster, climbed out, and flew again the same night.

On her most intense night, Popova flew 18 consecutive bombing missions — nearly nine straight hours of flying into anti-aircraft fire and back, over and over. When asked years later how she kept going, she said simply: "I sometimes stare into the blackness and close my eyes. I can still imagine myself as a young girl, up there in my little bomber. And I ask myself, 'Nadia, how did you do it?'"

## Twenty-Three Heroes

By the time the war ended in May 1945, the 588th Night Bomber Regiment had flown more than 23,000 combat sorties. They'd dropped over 3,000 tons of bombs and 26,000 incendiary shells on German positions. They had become the most highly decorated female unit in the entire Soviet Air Force.

Twenty-three of the Night Witches were awarded the title Hero of the Soviet Union — the highest distinction in the country. Many individual pilots had flown over 800 missions.

And yet, when the Soviet victory parade was held in Moscow, the Night Witches were not invited. Their Po-2 biplanes were deemed too slow to fly in the aerial display. The regiment was disbanded, and many of the women went back to their pre-war lives — students, teachers, engineers — carrying medals and memories that the world would largely forget for decades.

## The Sound That Lingers

There's something about this story that refuses to stay in the past. Maybe it's the absurdity of the image: teenage girls in canvas biplanes, no parachutes, engine off, gliding through searchlight beams in the freezing dark to drop two bombs on a professional army. Maybe it's the math — 23,000 sorties, one plywood plane at a time. Maybe it's the fact that they were erased from the victory celebration of the very war they helped win.

Or maybe it's that sound. The Germans heard broomsticks. But what it really was — the sound of a biplane gliding without power through the night sky — was the sound of people who had nothing doing everything with it.

Nadezhda Popova died in 2013 at the age of 91, one of the last surviving Night Witches. She'd spent a lifetime being asked the same question. Her answer never changed: *"I sometimes stare into the darkness and close my eyes, and I'm back there again."*

The darkness remembers.
