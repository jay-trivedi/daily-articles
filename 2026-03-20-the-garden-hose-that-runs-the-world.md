# The Garden Hose That Runs the World

*March 20, 2026 — Technology*

Right now, as you read this, your data is almost certainly traveling through a tube no thicker than a garden hose, sitting on the floor of an ocean, in complete darkness, under pressures that would crush a submarine. And that tube — along with about 600 others like it — carries over 97% of all intercontinental internet traffic on Earth.

Not satellites. Not some invisible wireless miracle. Actual physical cables, draped across the seabed like the world's most important extension cords.

## A Rich Man's Obsession

The story of undersea cables begins with a man who had already won at life and got bored. Cyrus West Field was a New York paper manufacturer who retired at the age of 33 with a quarter-million dollars in the bank — roughly $9 million in today's money. In January 1854, a British engineer named Frederic Gisborne walked into his life with a modest proposal: connect Newfoundland to New York by telegraph. Field looked at a globe in his Gramercy Park home and had a thought that would consume the next twelve years of his life: *Why stop at New York? Why not keep going all the way to Ireland?*

At the time, this was roughly as practical as suggesting you build a bridge to the moon.

The Atlantic Ocean is 3,000 kilometers of freezing water, crushing depths, and unpredictable currents. Nobody had ever laid a cable longer than a few hundred miles. The engineering didn't exist. The materials barely existed. But Field had money, charm, and the kind of stubbornness that history either calls genius or madness, depending on whether you succeed.

He secured financing from British and American investors, got backing from both governments, and in 1857, two ships — the USS *Niagara* and HMS *Agamemnon* — set out to lay a cable from Ireland to Newfoundland. The first attempt broke after 6 miles. They tried again the next day and got 80 miles before it snapped. Two days later, they managed 225 miles before the signal died. The sensible thing would have been to go home. Field pointed out they still had cable and a month of summer left. They tried again.

On August 16, 1858, Queen Victoria sent President James Buchanan a message in Morse code. The celebrations were extraordinary — New York held a parade, fireworks erupted, church bells rang. A message that once took ten days by ship now took minutes.

Three weeks later, the cable went dead.

It took Field eight more years to try again. In 1866, the massive ship *Great Eastern* — at the time the largest vessel ever built — finally laid a cable that held. The age of instant global communication had begun, and it started at the bottom of the ocean.

## Thinner Than You Think

Modern submarine cables would be almost comically unimpressive if you held one in your hand. The deep-ocean sections are about 17 to 20 millimeters in diameter — genuinely the width of a garden hose. Inside that modest tube are hair-thin strands of silica glass fiber, wrapped in layers of steel wire, copper conductor, and polyethylene sheathing.

But what those hair-thin fibers can do is staggering. Using a technique called Dense Wavelength Division Multiplexing (DWDM), a single fiber can carry multiple wavelengths of light simultaneously — each wavelength a separate data stream. Modern cables carry hundreds of terabits per second. Google's "Grace Hopper" cable, connecting the US, UK, and Spain, uses 16 fiber pairs and carries enough bandwidth to stream millions of HD videos at once.

The real engineering miracle, though, sits every 40 to 80 kilometers along the cable: the repeater. These are sealed metal cylinders — usually beryllium copper — containing erbium-doped fiber amplifiers that boost the light signal as it weakens. They must operate at depths of 4,000 to 8,000 meters, under pressures exceeding 1,000 atmospheres, at near-freezing temperatures, for a design lifetime of **25 years** — with zero maintenance. You can't exactly send a technician to the bottom of the Pacific.

To handle the inevitable failure of components, repeaters use elegant redundancy: four pump lasers share the load, and the system can keep running even if three of the four fail. The entire cable is powered from both ends simultaneously, with about 20,000 volts split down the middle, so if one shore station loses power, the other can keep every repeater on the line alive.

## The Numbers Are Absurd

As of 2026, roughly 600 submarine cables crisscross the world's oceans, spanning about 1.4 million kilometers. That's enough to wrap around the Earth 35 times. This year alone, nearly 40 new cables are projected to come online — representing about $6 billion in investment — the highest number in at least a decade, driven largely by the insatiable bandwidth demands of cloud computing and AI.

And here's the part that should make you slightly uneasy: four companies — Meta, Google, Amazon, and Microsoft — now control more than 70% of the world's transcontinental cable networks. The backbone of global communication, the physical infrastructure that connects continents, is increasingly owned by the same companies that dominate what flows through it.

## Sharks, Anchors, and the Fragility of Everything

You've probably heard that sharks bite submarine cables. It's a great story. In the mid-1980s, deep-dwelling crocodile sharks near the Canary Islands did indeed chew through the polyethylene sheathing on some early fiber-optic cables — likely attracted by the electromagnetic fields. Google even wraps some of its cables in Kevlar-like protective yarn partly for this reason.

But the truth is less cinematic. Shark bites accounted for less than 1% of cable faults before 2006, and since then, exactly zero have been recorded. The real threats are far more mundane: fishing trawlers and ship anchors cause 65 to 75% of all cable damage. Submarine cables break about 200 times per year, and a fleet of specialized repair ships sits scattered across the world's oceans, essentially on permanent standby, waiting for the next rupture. Each repair costs $1 to $2 million.

The vulnerability is real. In 2008, when two cables were cut in the Mediterranean near Alexandria, Egypt lost 70% of its internet connectivity. In February 2024, Houthi attacks in the Red Sea damaged three cables carrying about 25% of traffic between Asia and Europe. A well-placed anchor in the wrong spot can cut off millions of people from the global internet.

## The Invisible Foundation

There's something philosophically fascinating about the fact that the most advanced communication system in human history — the infrastructure that enables everything from AI to cryptocurrency to the video call with your grandmother — is, at its core, just light bouncing through glass tubes lying on the ocean floor.

We talk endlessly about "the cloud" as if our data floats in some ethereal digital heaven. It doesn't. It sinks. All the way to the bottom of the sea, where it races between continents at the speed of light through cables no thicker than your thumb, maintained by nothing but clever engineering and the hope that no one drags an anchor through the wrong patch of ocean.

Cyrus Field would probably find this hilarious. He spent twelve years and his entire fortune to send a single Morse code message across the Atlantic. Today, the descendants of his cable carry the entire internet. The ambition hasn't changed — just the bandwidth.
