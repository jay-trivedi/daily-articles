# The Empire That Drowned in Silver — How the World's Richest Nation Went Bankrupt

In 1545, an indigenous herder named Diego Huallpa was chasing a runaway llama up a mountain in what is now Bolivia. As the story goes, he stumbled, grabbed a shrub to steady himself, and yanked it out of the ground — roots and all. Clinging to those roots was a clump of pure silver so rich it glittered in the Andean sun.

That mountain was Cerro Rico. The Rich Mountain. And it would reshape the entire world.

## A Mountain Made of Money

Within months of Huallpa's discovery, Spanish colonists descended on the site like flies to sugar. What they found was staggering: the mountain wasn't just *near* silver deposits — it practically *was* one. Cerro Rico would go on to produce an estimated 60% of all the silver mined on Earth during the second half of the 16th century. Between the 1500s and 1700s, roughly 80% of the world's silver supply flowed out of this single mountain.

The city that sprang up at its base, Potosí, exploded in size. By 1600, it had a population of around 160,000 — larger than London, larger than Paris, one of the biggest cities in the world. It had theatres, gambling houses, churches with silver altars, and a mint that stamped out coins destined for every corner of the globe. A common Spanish saying became: *"vale un Potosí"* — "it's worth a Potosí" — the equivalent of saying something was worth a fortune.

Spain had won the lottery. But the lottery, it turned out, was cursed.

## The Mountain That Eats Men

Before we follow the silver to Spain, we need to talk about how it was extracted — because the human cost was monstrous.

The Spanish adapted the Inca *mita* system, a form of rotational labor tribute, and weaponized it. Under the colonial mita, indigenous communities for hundreds of miles across the altiplano were required to send one-seventh of their adult male population to the mines every year. These *mitayos* would descend into tunnels that grew deeper, hotter, and more suffocating with each passing decade.

The conditions were medieval nightmares. Temperatures underground were brutal. The air was thick with oil-lamp smoke and rock dust. Miners broke rock by hand and hauled heavy bags of ore up ladders made of frayed rope and rotting wood. When the Spanish introduced mercury amalgamation to extract silver more efficiently in the 1570s, they added mercury poisoning to the list of ways the mountain killed you.

The locals had a name for Cerro Rico: *"the mountain that eats men."* Estimates of the total death toll range from hundreds of thousands to as many as eight million over the colonial period — though the higher figure likely includes deaths across the entire Viceroyalty of Peru. Whatever the exact number, the scale of suffering was immense. Potosí's silver was extracted not just from rock, but from human lives.

## The Richest Empire on Earth (On Paper)

Back in Spain, the treasure fleets were arriving. Convoy after convoy crossed the Atlantic, their holds groaning with silver bars and coins. At its peak in the 1580s and 1590s, silver taxes accounted for over 40% of the Spanish Crown's income. King Philip II ruled an empire on which the sun never set — Spain, the Netherlands, much of Italy, the Philippines, and half of the Americas.

Philip spent accordingly. He funded the construction of the massive El Escorial palace. He assembled the Spanish Armada against England. He waged war in the Netherlands, in France, in the Mediterranean against the Ottomans. Spain was fighting on every front simultaneously, and silver was the fuel.

But here's the thing about having a seemingly infinite supply of money: you start to spend like it actually is infinite. Philip II's military campaigns were breathtakingly expensive. At its worst, war spending consumed as much as 90% of the Crown's budget. Spain's total debt ballooned to 85.5 million ducats — against an annual income of just 9.7 million.

The math didn't work. Despite sitting atop the richest silver mines in human history, Philip II declared bankruptcy four times: in 1557, 1560, 1575, and 1596. The richest monarch in the world kept running out of money.

## The Curse Nobody Saw Coming

The deeper problem wasn't just overspending — it was something economists wouldn't have a name for until the 20th century: Dutch disease.

Here's how it worked. As mountains of silver poured into Spain, prices rose. And rose. And kept rising. The Castilian price index climbed 53% between 1566 and 1600 alone. Over the full 16th century, commodity prices across Spain increased by roughly 400%. This was the Price Revolution — the first major inflationary crisis in European history, and Spain was ground zero.

With prices soaring at home, Spanish goods became expensive compared to foreign products. Why buy Spanish cloth when English or Dutch cloth was cheaper? Spanish exports became uncompetitive. Domestic manufacturing withered. Agriculture stagnated. Why invest in building a textile mill or improving a farm when you could just import what you needed with silver?

Spain was experiencing, in real time, what happens when a country gets rich from extraction instead of production. The silver didn't build an industrial base. It didn't create a skilled workforce. It didn't fund innovation. It flowed through Spain like water through a sieve — arriving from the Americas, passing briefly through Spanish hands, and ending up in the pockets of Genoese bankers, Dutch merchants, English manufacturers, and Chinese traders who actually *made* the things Spain was buying.

The nations that sold goods to Spain were the ones getting richer. Spain was just the middleman with a mountain.

## The Unraveling

By the early 1600s, the cracks were everywhere. Silver production at Potosí began declining as the easiest veins were exhausted. The Thirty Years' War (1618–1648) bled Spain of men and money. The Dutch Republic — once a Spanish possession — had broken free and was building a genuine commercial empire based on trade, finance, and manufacturing, not extraction.

The final symbolic blow came in 1659, when the Treaty of the Pyrenees confirmed France's ascendancy over Spain as Europe's dominant power. Within a century of its peak, the Spanish Empire had been overtaken by nations that had started with far less silver but had invested in far more sustainable forms of wealth.

By the time the Bourbon dynasty inherited the Spanish throne in 1700, they inherited an empire that was vast in territory but hollow in substance — a cautionary tale already centuries old.

## The Lesson in the Mountain

There's a term in economics called the "resource curse" — the paradox that countries with abundant natural resources often end up with slower economic growth, more corruption, and weaker institutions than countries without them. Venezuela and oil. The Congo and minerals. The pattern repeats.

Spain wrote the original version of that story. Cerro Rico didn't just eat men — it ate an empire.

The mountain still stands today, in modern-day Bolivia. It's visibly smaller than it was in 1545, hollowed out by five centuries of mining. Miners still work its tunnels, many of them children, using methods that would be recognizable to the mitayos of Philip II's era. The city of Potosí, once one of the largest and wealthiest in the world, is now one of the poorest in South America.

And somewhere in the rubble of that shrinking mountain is a lesson that humanity keeps having to relearn: the most dangerous kind of wealth is the kind you didn't have to build.
