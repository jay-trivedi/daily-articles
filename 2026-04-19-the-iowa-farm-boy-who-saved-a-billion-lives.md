# The Iowa Farm Boy Who Saved a Billion Lives

In 1968, a U.S. government biologist named Paul Ehrlich published a book that became a sensation. *The Population Bomb* opened with a sentence as blunt as a gunshot: "The battle to feed all of humanity is over." India, Ehrlich declared, was doomed. "I don't see how India could possibly feed two hundred million more people by 1980." Famine was inevitable. Hundreds of millions would starve. Triage the survivors, he advised. Let the rest go.

Almost nobody noticed that on the other side of the world, a stocky, sunburnt, foul-mouthed wheat breeder from Iowa was already proving him wrong — with a plant.

## The kid who almost wasn't a scientist

Norman Ernest Borlaug was born on March 25, 1914, on a small Norwegian-immigrant farm in Cresco, Iowa. He grew up pitching hay, milking cows, wrestling at the one-room schoolhouse, and absorbing his grandfather's advice: *"Fill your head now to fill your belly later."* He drove to the University of Minnesota in 1933 planning to become a high-school teacher and almost flunked the entrance exam. He paid his way through by waiting tables, joining Civilian Conservation Corps crews, and — once — running out of money and having to drop out for a semester to work at a forestry camp, where he saw starving men so close to death they ate grass.

He never forgot their faces.

He got a doctorate in plant pathology in 1942, turned down a cushy DuPont job after Pearl Harbor (they reinstated him; he left anyway), and in 1944 accepted a Rockefeller Foundation posting most American scientists considered a career graveyard: rural Mexico, to study a fungus called stem rust that was ravaging wheat.

## A rust-eaten country

When Borlaug arrived in the Yaqui Valley, Mexican wheat farmers were harvesting fewer bushels per acre than farmers had in the Middle Ages. Fields rotted black with rust. The country imported half its bread. Borlaug worked in shorts and sandals, often without a tractor. To speed up breeding, he did something other scientists said was impossible: he moved his nursery between two fields 700 miles apart and 10,000 feet different in elevation — the hot Sonoran lowlands in winter, the cool central highlands in summer. The technique was called "shuttle breeding," and it let him grow two generations of wheat a year instead of one. It also accidentally produced varieties that thrived across a huge range of latitudes and day-lengths — a property that would later matter enormously.

By the mid-1950s, he had varieties resistant to multiple rust strains. But they had a problem. When Mexican farmers dumped fertilizer on them, the plants shot up, grew fat heads of grain — and then toppled over under their own weight. The harvest rotted in the mud.

The fix came from a short Japanese wheat called *Norin 10*, which Borlaug crossed into his lines starting in 1953. The offspring were semi-dwarfs — squat, stiff-stemmed plants that could hold huge heads of grain without falling over. They were ugly. Old-school agronomists sneered. They also produced two to three times the grain of traditional varieties.

## The freighter race

By 1963, Mexico was a net wheat exporter. But India and Pakistan were starving. Monsoons had failed. American PL-480 grain ships were the only thing between the subcontinent and mass famine; at one point, one-fifth of all U.S. wheat was being shipped to India. President Lyndon Johnson was rationing it month to month as political leverage.

In 1965, after fierce bureaucratic battles with ministries that didn't want foreign seed, Borlaug shipped **450 tons of Lerma Rojo and Sonora 64 seed** — 250 to Pakistan, 200 to India. The shipment nearly died at the Mexican border when U.S. Marines shot warning rounds during the Watts riots and closed the highway. It arrived at the port late. Indo-Pakistani war broke out. Artillery could be heard from Borlaug's test plots outside Lahore.

He planted anyway.

The results weren't incremental. They were biblical. Pakistan's wheat harvest: **4.6 million tons in 1965 → 7.3 million tons in 1970**. India's: **12.3 million tons → 20.1 million tons** in the same five years. By 1968, Pakistan was self-sufficient. By 1974, India was self-sufficient in *all* cereals. The famines Ehrlich had declared inevitable simply never happened.

In 1970, Borlaug got a phone call at 4 a.m. in a wheat field outside Mexico City. His wife had driven out to find him. He had won the Nobel Peace Prize — the first agricultural scientist ever to receive one. He was annoyed; he wanted to finish the day's crossings. He gave a short speech, then went back to the field.

## The number nobody can quite prove

How many lives did he save? The most-cited estimate — one billion — comes from a calculation by the agricultural economist M. S. Swaminathan and others: counting the population that existed on land that could not, without semi-dwarf wheat, have fed them. The number is deliberately provocative. Even the conservative estimates run into the hundreds of millions. No individual in human history has a stronger case for having personally prevented more deaths.

## The shadow

The Green Revolution has critics, and they are not wrong. The new wheat demanded irrigation, nitrogen fertilizer, and pesticides — inputs that favored richer farmers, drained aquifers (Punjab's groundwater collapse is directly traceable to this), and built the modern monoculture. Smallholders were squeezed. Soils hardened. The ecological bill is still being paid.

Borlaug knew. In his Nobel lecture, he said plainly: "The Green Revolution has won a temporary success in man's war against hunger. It has given man a breathing space." He meant a *breathing space* — not a solution. If humanity didn't also control population growth, he warned, the respite would be squandered. He spent the rest of his life pushing for better nitrogen efficiency, higher-yield rice, and — despite fierce opposition from European NGOs he came to despise — transgenic crops for Africa.

## The quiet death of a giant

Borlaug died in Dallas on September 12, 2009, at 95, of lymphoma. He had kept working until months before — flying to drought-hit regions, badgering ministers, breeding wheat. His last reported words to his family were: *"Take it to the farmer."*

Ehrlich, at the time of writing, is still alive. He has never retracted *The Population Bomb*.

Most people you pass on the street today owe their existence, in some statistical sense, to a short, cranky Iowa farm boy who spent his life stooped in a field, crossing one wheat plant with another, because he once saw hungry men eat grass.

That is not a metaphor. It happened. And it is, arguably, the single most consequential act of human kindness ever performed.
