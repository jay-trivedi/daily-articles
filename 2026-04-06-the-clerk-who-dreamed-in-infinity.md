# The Clerk Who Dreamed in Infinity

On January 16, 1913, a letter arrived at Trinity College, Cambridge, addressed to G.H. Hardy — one of England's finest mathematicians. It was written by a 25-year-old clerk from Madras who earned 20 pounds a year, had no university degree, and had taught himself mathematics from a single outdated textbook. The letter contained nine pages of formulas. Some Hardy recognized. Some he'd never seen before. A few, he later admitted, "defeated me completely."

That clerk's name was Srinivasa Ramanujan. And those nine pages would change mathematics forever.

## A Mind That Didn't Wait for Permission

Ramanujan was born on December 22, 1887, in Erode, a small town in Tamil Nadu, and grew up in Kumbakonam — a place known more for its temples than its theorems. His father was a clerk at a cloth merchant's shop. His mother sang devotional songs at the local temple. Nothing about his circumstances suggested that one of the greatest mathematical minds in history was incubating in a modest South Indian household.

By age 12, he had mastered trigonometry that college students struggled with. At 15, he got his hands on a copy of *A Synopsis of Elementary Results in Pure and Applied Mathematics* by G.S. Carr — a dry, formula-heavy reference book with over 5,000 results listed without proofs. Most students would have used it as a doorstop. Ramanujan treated it like a puzzle box. He worked through formula after formula, not just verifying them but extending them, discovering patterns Carr had never imagined.

Here's the thing: because the book listed results without proofs, Ramanujan developed the habit of arriving at truths through intuition and then working backwards to justify them — or sometimes not bothering to justify them at all. This would later drive professional mathematicians absolutely mad. His notebooks were forests of results with barely a breadcrumb trail of reasoning.

He won a scholarship to the Government Arts College in Kumbakonam but promptly lost it — he was so obsessed with mathematics that he neglected every other subject and failed his exams. Twice. He tried another college in Madras. Same result. The greatest mathematical genius of his generation was, on paper, a college dropout.

## Nine Pages That Changed Everything

Unable to find academic work, Ramanujan took a job as an accounting clerk at the Madras Port Trust. But he kept filling notebooks. By 1912, a few local mathematicians who had seen his work urged him to write to someone in England who might understand what he had.

He wrote to three Cambridge mathematicians. Two ignored him. The third was G.H. Hardy.

Hardy almost threw the letter away. Unsolicited mathematical manuscripts from unknown Indians were not exactly rare, and most were crankery. But something stopped him. He sat down with his colleague J.E. Littlewood after dinner that night and they began working through Ramanujan's formulas.

Some were already known results that Ramanujan had independently rediscovered — impressive but not earth-shattering. Some were wrong. But others were things Hardy had never seen, things that seemed to come from nowhere, things that were so strange and beautiful that they could not have been invented by a fraud. As Hardy later wrote: "They must be true, because if they were not true, no one would have had the imagination to invent them."

Hardy rated mathematicians on a scale of 0 to 100. He gave himself 25. He gave Littlewood 30. He gave David Hilbert — the titan of early 20th-century math — 80. He gave Ramanujan 100.

## Cambridge, Cold Weather, and Collaboration

Hardy arranged for Ramanujan to come to Cambridge. In the spring of 1914, Ramanujan arrived — a strict vegetarian Brahmin in a land of boiled mutton, a man from tropical South India thrust into the bone-chilling damp of an English winter. He moved into rooms in Whewell's Court at Trinity College.

What followed was one of the most extraordinary collaborations in mathematical history. Hardy was rigorous, formal, proof-obsessed. Ramanujan was intuitive, leaping to conclusions that took Hardy months to verify. Together, they were electric.

Their most celebrated joint work was on the partition function — the deceptively simple question of how many ways you can write a number as a sum of positive integers. The number 4, for instance, can be partitioned five ways: 4, 3+1, 2+2, 2+1+1, and 1+1+1+1. Simple enough. But by the time you reach 200, the number of partitions explodes to 3,972,999,029,388. Hardy and Ramanujan developed an astonishing formula that could calculate partition numbers with extraordinary accuracy, using a technique that Hardy called "one of the most remarkable in all of mathematics."

Then there was the taxicab number. When Ramanujan was ill in a London hospital, Hardy came to visit and mentioned that the cab he'd arrived in was numbered 1729 — "a rather dull number," Hardy remarked. Ramanujan's eyes lit up. "No, Hardy," he said. "It is a very interesting number. It is the smallest number expressible as the sum of two cubes in two different ways." (1729 = 1³ + 12³ = 9³ + 10³.) He hadn't looked this up. He simply *knew*. Numbers spoke to him the way colors speak to painters.

## The Letter from the Edge

The English climate was devastating for Ramanujan's health. He was diagnosed with tuberculosis (though modern scholars suspect a parasitic liver infection). In 1919, gravely ill, he returned to India.

But even on what was essentially his deathbed, Ramanujan kept working. On January 12, 1920 — just months before his death — he wrote one final letter to Hardy. In it, he described a new class of functions he called "mock theta functions." He listed 17 examples. He gave no proofs. He gave no definitions. He gave almost no explanation of what they were or why they mattered. It was classic Ramanujan: here are the answers, the questions are your problem.

He died on April 26, 1920, at the age of 32.

For decades, those mock theta functions remained a mystery. Mathematicians could verify that Ramanujan's examples worked, but nobody could figure out the underlying theory — what connected them, what made them tick. It wasn't until 2002 that Sander Zwegers finally cracked the code, showing that mock theta functions were pieces of something called harmonic Maass forms. Ramanujan had glimpsed, through pure intuition, a mathematical structure that the field wouldn't formally understand for another 82 years.

## The Lost Notebook

The story gets stranger. In 1976, mathematician George Andrews was poking around the Wren Library at Trinity College when he found a box of papers belonging to G.N. Watson, a mathematician who had studied Ramanujan's work. Inside were 138 loose sheets covered in Ramanujan's distinctive handwriting — over 600 formulas, none with proofs. This was Ramanujan's "lost notebook," containing the mathematics of his final year of life.

The lost notebook has been yielding discoveries for half a century now. And as recently as December 2025, researchers at IISc Bangalore and other institutions demonstrated that Ramanujan's century-old pi formulas — those strange infinite series he wrote down without explanation — appear naturally within logarithmic conformal field theories, a framework used to describe phenomena from turbulence to black holes. The same mathematical structures Ramanujan intuited while working as a clerk in Madras turn out to be embedded in the equations governing the most extreme objects in the universe.

## What Do We Make of This?

Ramanujan left behind roughly 3,900 results, most without proof. Virtually all of them have since been verified as correct. He worked without the internet, without a university library, without colleagues, and — for most of his life — without anyone who could even understand what he was doing.

Hardy, who was famously atheist, once said that if he had to describe Ramanujan's mathematical abilities in one word, the word would be "natural." Other great mathematicians could be explained by talent plus hard work. Ramanujan seemed to simply *see* mathematics, the way the rest of us see the physical world.

When asked how he arrived at his results, Ramanujan would sometimes say that the goddess Namagiri whispered formulas to him in dreams. Hardy found this exasperating. But here we are, over a century later, still unpacking the dreams — and finding that they describe black holes and the deep structure of reality.

Some things, it turns out, are worth dreaming about.
