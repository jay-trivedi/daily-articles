# The 2,500-Mile Wall of Thorns That Britain Built Through the Heart of India

Imagine a hedge. Not the knee-high boxwood edging your neighbour's lawn, but a monstrous, twelve-foot wall of thorns — impenetrable, continuous, and patrolled by armed guards every few hundred yards. Now imagine that hedge stretching for two thousand five hundred miles, from the salt flats of the Punjab down through the plains of central India, cutting the subcontinent roughly in half. And imagine that this hedge existed for one purpose: to make sure poor people paid tax on salt.

This was the Great Hedge of India. And until a London librarian stumbled across a passing mention of it in 1995, it had been almost completely forgotten.

## A Tax on Sweat

The story begins with a simple economic reality: India's western and southern coasts have abundant natural salt. The interior does not. When the British East India Company consolidated its grip on Bengal in the late eighteenth century, it spotted an opportunity. Salt produced cheaply on the coast could be taxed heavily before it reached the millions of people inland who desperately needed it. By controlling the flow of salt, the British could generate enormous revenue with minimal effort.

There was just one problem. India is not an island. Salt could be carried across borders, smuggled on bullock carts, hidden in grain sacks, tucked into turbans. The land boundary between cheap-salt territory and expensive-salt territory was thousands of miles of open countryside — no mountains, no rivers, nothing to stop a determined smuggler with a donkey and a sack of crystals.

So the British decided to build a wall.

## From Customs Houses to Living Barrier

It started modestly. In 1803, the East India Company established a series of customs houses in Bengal to intercept salt smugglers. Over the next four decades, these houses were linked together and extended, forming a continuous bureaucratic barrier — the Inland Customs Line — that was brought under a single department in 1843.

At first, the barrier was crude: ditches, earthen banks, piles of dried thorny brush stacked like barbed wire. Patrols walked the line. Smugglers were caught, fined, imprisoned. But the line was leaky. Dried brush rotted. Storms scattered it. White ants devoured it from within. Every monsoon season, entire sections had to be rebuilt from scratch.

Then, in 1867, a man named Allan Octavian Hume was appointed Commissioner of Inland Customs. Hume — who would later go on to co-found the Indian National Congress — was a meticulous administrator, and he had an idea. Instead of constantly rebuilding dead barriers, why not grow a living one?

Hume began trials of indigenous thorny shrubs: Indian plum, babool, karonda, several species of the viciously spiny Euphorbia. He tested which species tolerated which soils, which rainfall, which altitudes. By 1868, 180 miles of the line had become, in official reports, "thoroughly impenetrable" living hedge. Hume's men kept planting. In 1869 alone, they dug two million cubic feet of earth and hauled over 150,000 tons of thorny material into place.

By the mid-1870s, the Inland Customs Line had reached its grotesque peak: 2,504 miles of continuous barrier, running from the Indus River in the northwest to the Mahanadi in Odisha. More than half of it was mature, green, living hedge — in places twelve feet tall and fourteen feet thick, so dense that not even a dog could push through. The rest was packed dry thorn. Along the entire length, chaukis — guard posts — stood every three or four hundred yards, manned by armed chaukidars. There were 1,727 of these posts. The line employed over 12,000 men. It was, by any measure, one of the largest structures ever built — longer than the Great Wall of China, though far less durable.

## The Cruelty of the Thing

The hedge was not a military fortification. It was not built to repel invaders. It existed to ensure that the poorest people in one of the poorest countries on earth paid an inflated price for a substance their bodies literally could not survive without.

Salt is not a luxury. It is a biological necessity. In the blistering heat of the Indian plains, where labourers sweated through twelve-hour days in the fields, salt loss was constant and dangerous. Without it, muscles cramp, the heart falters, the mind clouds. The British knew this. They taxed it anyway.

The salt tax made the price of salt in British-controlled India ten to twenty times higher than in surrounding regions. For a labourer earning a few annas a day, this was devastating. Families went without. People sickened. During the famines that periodically swept India in the nineteenth century — famines that British policies themselves often worsened — salt deprivation compounded starvation, killing people who might otherwise have survived on what little grain they had.

By 1877, the salt tax was pouring £6.3 million a year into British coffers. The hedge was doing its job.

## The Disappearing Act

And then, almost as abruptly as it had grown, the hedge was abandoned. In 1879, the British seized control of the Sambhar Salt Lake in Rajasthan — one of India's largest inland salt sources — and decided it was easier to tax salt at the point of production rather than police its movement across two and a half thousand miles. The Inland Customs Line was dismantled. The 12,000 guards were reassigned or dismissed. The hedge was left to the elements.

Within a few decades, it had vanished. The thorns rotted, were eaten by termites, were cleared by farmers reclaiming land. The earthen banks eroded. The guard posts crumbled. By the early twentieth century, almost no trace remained.

But the salt tax did not disappear with the hedge. It persisted for another sixty-seven years, grinding quietly on, extracting wealth from the poorest Indians. It became, in a sense, invisible — which is perhaps what made it such a perfect target for a man who understood the power of making injustice visible.

## A Pinch of Salt That Shook an Empire

On March 12, 1930, a sixty-year-old man in a loincloth set out from his ashram in Ahmedabad with seventy-eight followers. They walked 240 miles in twenty-four days to the coastal village of Dandi, where, at 8:30 on the morning of April 6, Mohandas Gandhi bent down, picked up a lump of natural salt from the mud flats, and broke the law.

The salt tax, Gandhi had declared, was "the most inhuman poll tax the ingenuity of man can devise." His Salt March did not mention the Great Hedge — by 1930, it had been forgotten for half a century. But the march was aimed at the same fundamental injustice the hedge had been built to enforce: the idea that an empire could stand between a people and the earth beneath their feet, and charge them for the privilege of survival.

Millions followed. The British arrested over 60,000 people. The Raj never fully recovered.

## The Librarian Who Found the Ghost

The most remarkable footnote to this story belongs to Roy Moxham, a conservator at the University of London library. In 1995, while reading Major-General Sir William Henry Sleeman's *Rambles and Recollections of an Indian Official* — a book published in 1844 — Moxham came across a brief, puzzling reference to a great customs hedge in India. He had never heard of such a thing. No one he asked had either.

Moxham became obsessed. He dug through colonial archives, piecing together the story of the Inland Customs Line. Then, in 1998, he travelled to India to find the hedge itself. What he discovered, after months of searching, was heartbreaking in its ordinariness: a small, raised embankment in the Etawah district of Uttar Pradesh. A faint ridge in the dirt. That was all that remained of a structure that had once employed more men than most armies, stretched longer than the Great Wall, and helped starve millions.

Moxham published his findings in 2001 as *The Great Hedge of India*. The book is part detective story, part colonial history, and entirely devastating.

## What the Thorns Remember

There is something almost too perfect about a forgotten hedge. The Great Wall of China endures as a tourist attraction. Hadrian's Wall is a hiking trail. But the Great Hedge of India — a structure of comparable ambition and far greater cruelty — has returned to the earth. It left no ruins to photograph, no stones to climb, no gift shops. It exists now only in the archives it was designed to fill with revenue figures, and in the bodies of the people it was designed to impoverish.

Perhaps that is the most honest monument an empire can leave behind: not a wall of stone, but a wall of thorns that grew, did its damage, and rotted away — leaving nothing but a faint ridge in the soil and the question of how something so vast could be so thoroughly erased.
