# The Clock That Proved Einstein Right — and Now Holds Civilization Together

In October 1971, a physicist named Joseph Hafele and an astronomer named Richard Keating walked onto a series of commercial Pan Am flights carrying four cesium atomic clocks in their own purchased seats. The clocks had boarding passes. The flight attendants were confused.

The plan was beautifully simple and slightly insane: fly around the entire world twice — once eastward, once westward — then compare the traveling clocks against identical ones sitting quietly at the United States Naval Observatory in Washington, D.C. If Einstein was right about relativity, the clocks should disagree when they were reunited. Time itself should have passed at different rates depending on speed and altitude.

The eastward trip lasted 65.4 hours. The westward trip, 80.3. And when Hafele and Keating brought their clocks home and lined them up against the stationary reference?

The eastward clocks had lost 59 nanoseconds. The westward clocks had gained 273 nanoseconds.

Fifty-nine billionths of a second. That's how precisely these clocks measured the warping of time aboard a commercial airliner. And the numbers matched Einstein's predictions almost exactly.

But to understand why this mattered — and why it still matters every time you open Google Maps — you have to go back to a lab in Teddington, England, sixteen years earlier.

## The Man Who Redefined a Second

In 1955, a physicist named Louis Essen at the National Physical Laboratory did something quietly revolutionary: he built the first practical atomic clock.

Essen had spent years working with quartz crystal oscillators, but he knew they weren't good enough. Quartz clocks drifted. The Earth's own rotation, which had defined the second for all of human history, was even worse — it wobbled, slowed down, sped up with tidal forces. Essen wanted something that didn't care about weather, gravity, or geology. He wanted a clock that ticked to the rhythm of the universe itself.

He found it in cesium.

Cesium-133 has a single outer electron that flips between two energy states when exposed to microwave radiation at a very specific frequency: 9,192,631,770 cycles per second. That number isn't approximate. It isn't rounded. It's a property of the atom itself — the same in London, on Mars, or at the edge of a black hole.

On May 24, 1955, Essen and his colleague Jack Parry switched on their cesium clock for the first time. It was accurate to about one second in 300 years — ludicrously, almost absurdly better than anything that had come before.

In 1967, the world took the hint. The General Conference on Weights and Measures officially redefined the second: no longer a fraction of the Earth's rotation, but precisely 9,192,631,770 oscillations of a cesium-133 atom. For the first time in history, time was no longer measured by looking at the sky. It was measured by listening to atoms.

## The 30-Centimeter Problem

Here is a fact that should unsettle you: GPS only works because we take Einstein's relativity into account.

Each GPS satellite carries atomic clocks. Your phone picks up signals from at least four of them, measures the tiny differences in arrival time, and triangulates your position. The math is straightforward, but the precision required is ferocious.

Light travels about 30 centimeters in one nanosecond. So a timing error of just one nanosecond means your position is off by a foot. A timing error of one microsecond — one-millionth of a second — throws you off by 300 meters. An error of one millisecond would put you in a different neighborhood. Without correction, GPS errors would accumulate at roughly 10 kilometers per day.

And here's the kicker: the clocks on GPS satellites run at a different rate than clocks on the ground.

General relativity says clocks tick faster when gravity is weaker — up in orbit, 20,200 kilometers above the Earth, the satellites' clocks gain about 45 microseconds per day. Special relativity says clocks tick slower when they're moving fast — the satellites are zipping along at 14,000 kilometers per hour, which costs them about 7 microseconds per day. The net effect: the satellite clocks run 38 microseconds fast every day.

If the GPS engineers hadn't corrected for this, your position would drift by 10 kilometers daily. Within a week, your navigation app would think you were in the ocean. Einstein's "thought experiment" about clocks and gravity isn't philosophy. It's the firmware update that keeps your Uber arriving at the right address.

## The Invisible Backbone

But GPS is just the beginning. Atomic clocks quietly hold together systems you never think about.

In telecommunications, the signals carrying your phone calls and video streams are interleaved and separated based on precise timing. If the clocks at either end of a fiber-optic link drift apart by more than a few nanoseconds, data corrupts. Every cell tower, every internet exchange point, every undersea cable landing station is synchronized to atomic time.

In finance, trades on stock exchanges are timestamped to the microsecond. Regulators require firms to synchronize their clocks to detect market manipulation. High-frequency trading firms spend millions on timing infrastructure because a discrepancy of a few milliseconds is the difference between profit and loss.

Power grids use precise time signals to synchronize generators across a continent. Air traffic control depends on it. Scientific instruments from radio telescopes to particle accelerators are slaves to atomic time. The entire internet's security infrastructure — the certificates that make HTTPS work — depends on clocks agreeing about what time it is.

We built a civilization on nanoseconds, and most people have never heard of the machine that counts them.

## Clocks That Will Outlast the Stars

Louis Essen's 1955 cesium clock was accurate to one second in 300 years. Today's cesium clocks are accurate to one second in 300 million years. But even that is now considered crude.

The next generation of timekeeping uses optical lattice clocks. Instead of microwaves, they use visible light — with frequencies in the hundreds of trillions of hertz rather than billions, giving them far more "ticks" to count and far greater precision.

At JILA, a joint institute of NIST and the University of Colorado Boulder, researchers have built strontium optical lattice clocks with uncertainties below one part in 10^18. In practical terms: these clocks would not gain or lose a single second in 30 billion years. The universe is only 13.8 billion years old. These clocks are more accurate than the age of everything that exists.

In 2022, JILA physicists used these clocks to measure the time dilation caused by a height difference of just one millimeter — proving that time runs faster at the top of a tiny platform than at the bottom. Einstein's general relativity, confirmed across the width of a grain of rice.

Japan's Shimadzu Corporation began selling the first commercial optical lattice clock in 2025, for about $3.3 million per unit. The redefinition of the second — again — is expected within the decade.

## The Deepest Measurement

There's something almost philosophical about atomic clocks. We built them to measure time more precisely, and in doing so, we discovered that time itself isn't what we thought it was. It bends. It stretches. It runs at different speeds depending on where you are and how fast you're moving. The closer we look, the stranger it gets.

Two guys flew around the world with four clocks in airplane seats and proved that time is relative. Seventy years later, we can measure that relativity across a millimeter. And somewhere between those two experiments, we accidentally built a civilization that falls apart if our clocks are wrong by a billionth of a second.

We don't just measure time. We depend on it. And the machines that keep it are the most precise instruments human beings have ever built — ticking away in satellites and server rooms and government basements, quietly holding the modern world together, one atom at a time.
