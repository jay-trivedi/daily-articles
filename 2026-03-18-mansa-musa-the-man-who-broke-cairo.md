# The Man Who Broke Cairo's Economy Just by Being Generous

*March 18, 2026*

In July 1324, something unprecedented happened at the outskirts of Cairo. A king arrived.

Not unusual — Cairo was the world's greatest city, a hub of trade, scholarship, and power. Kings and emirs came and went. But this king had traveled from West Africa with 60,000 people, 500 slaves each carrying a six-pound gold staff, 80 camels each loaded with up to 300 pounds of gold dust, and an attitude of generosity so extravagant that he would accidentally destroy Egypt's economy for the next 12 years.

His name was Mansa Musa I, ruler of the Mali Empire, and he was on his way to Mecca.

---

## The Empire Behind the Man

To understand what happened in Cairo, you need to understand where Mansa Musa came from. "Mansa" means King of Kings — not a title given lightly. He ruled an empire that, at its height, stretched across 1.29 million square kilometers: parts of modern-day Mali, Senegal, Guinea, Mauritania, Burkina Faso, and Niger. In scale, it dwarfed most European kingdoms of the era.

The Mali Empire had two pillars: gold and salt. The goldfields of Bambuk, Bure, and Galam (across modern Guinea and Senegal) were the richest in the known world. At the empire's peak, **roughly half of all the gold circulating in the Old World** — in Europe, the Middle East, North Africa — was dug from Mali's mines and taxed by Mali's king. No single person before or since has controlled such a proportion of a globally essential commodity.

Salt came from the Saharan mines of Taghaza, deep in the desert. In much of sub-Saharan Africa, salt was so scarce that traders used slabs of it as currency. Mali controlled that too. There's a saying from the era: *"Salt comes from the north, gold from the south, and silver from the country of the white men — but the word of God and the treasures of wisdom are only to be found in Timbuktu."*

Timbuktu. Under Mansa Musa, this city on the southern edge of the Sahara had a population of around 100,000. Of those, roughly 25,000 were students and scholars — a full quarter of the city's population. The University of Sankore taught Islamic theology, mathematics, law, astronomy, medicine, and history. Foreign professors invited to teach there sometimes found themselves becoming the students. The institution was, by many accounts, the greatest center of Islamic learning in the medieval world. Hundreds of thousands of manuscripts were produced — many survive in private libraries in Mali to this day.

Mansa Musa's personal wealth? Historians describe it as literally incalculable. The 18 tons of gold he brought on a single trip — just the amount for one pilgrimage — is worth roughly $1.4 billion in today's prices. That was pocket money. Modern estimates of his total wealth range from $300 billion to $500 billion, though even those figures fail to capture what it means to personally control half the world's supply of its most essential trading commodity.

The Arab scholar Al-Umari, after exhaustive research, gave up trying to quantify it precisely and simply wrote: *"He is the most powerful, the richest, the most fortunate, the most blessed by good deeds, and the most powerful in armies, strength, and retinue."*

---

## The Pilgrimage

In 1324, Mansa Musa decided to fulfill the Islamic obligation of hajj — the pilgrimage to Mecca. He did not do this quietly.

His caravan departed from Niani, his capital on the Niger River. It crossed the Sahara via Taghaza, wound through central Algeria, and arrived at the pyramids of Giza. Then it crossed the Nile into Cairo proper on 19 July 1324.

The Mamluk Sultan Al-Nasir Muhammad received him. Accounts say Musa initially refused to prostrate before the Sultan — he would bow only before God, he said. Cairo's officials were reportedly not sure whether to be offended or impressed. In the end, diplomacy prevailed.

What happened next was the problem.

Mansa Musa gave. He gave to beggars in the street. He gave to religious figures and scholars. He gave to government officials, merchants, dignitaries, and anyone who crossed his path. He gave because generosity was not just a virtue to him — it was an expression of power, faith, and status. He gave so much that the streets of Cairo, then the largest gold market in the world, were suddenly flooded with the stuff.

Gold, like any commodity, obeys supply and demand. Before Musa's visit, one mithqal of gold (about 4.25 grams) was worth 25 silver dirhams. After a few weeks of Mansa Musa's generosity, it was worth fewer than 22 dirhams. A 12 percent drop in gold's value — and because gold was the basis of all other prices, that devaluation rippled through everything.

Merchants who had stored their wealth in gold suddenly found it worth less. Prices for goods and services rose. Contracts denominated in gold needed to be renegotiated. The Cairo gold market, the beating financial heart of the medieval world, went into shock.

Al-Umari, writing from direct accounts roughly 12 years later, noted that the Egyptian gold market was **still depressed** at the time of his writing. Twelve years. Because one man was too generous.

The effects didn't stop at Cairo. Because Cairo set the regional gold price, the damage spread across Mediterranean and Middle Eastern trading networks. Merchants from the Levant, from the Italian city-states, from Persia — anyone connected to the Old World's gold economy felt it.

---

## The Accidental Economist

Here's the remarkable coda: Mansa Musa realized what he'd done.

On his return journey through Cairo, having heard reports of the economic devastation trailing in his wake, he attempted to fix it. He began **buying back gold from Cairo's money-lenders at high interest rates** — deliberately withdrawing gold from circulation to restore its value. The strategy was correct. The richest man in history essentially conducted monetary policy from a camel.

It probably didn't fully work. But the attempt matters. This may be the earliest recorded instance of a deliberate intervention to counter inflation by reducing money supply. The man was 700 years ahead of central banking theory.

(There's also this wonderful detail: despite carrying 18 tons of gold, Mansa Musa ran short of cash during the journey and had to borrow money from Cairo merchants to fund the trip home. He later repaid at high interest. The richest person in history, taking out a loan. History has a sense of humor.)

## The Afterglow

The effects of Mansa Musa's hajj outlasted the man himself. In 1375 — fifty years after that Cairo visit — European cartographers were still so awed that the **Catalan Atlas**, one of the most important maps of the medieval world, depicts Mansa Musa seated on a golden throne, holding a gold orb and gold scepter, labeled the richest king on earth. This image circulated throughout Europe and the Mediterranean and shaped how educated Europeans imagined sub-Saharan Africa for generations.

He had also brought something home from Mecca: an Andalusian architect and poet named Abu Ishaq al-Sahili. In West Africa, most structures were built from mud and timber. Al-Sahili introduced fired brick and flat-roof construction. The mosques he designed — the Djinguereber and the Sankore in Timbuktu — are still standing after 700 years. They are UNESCO World Heritage Sites. Timbuktu's distinctive skyline, with wooden beams protruding from the walls (permanent scaffolding for annual replastering), is his legacy.

Mansa Musa died around 1337, and the decline began quickly. Weak successors, succession disputes, Tuareg raids, the rising Songhai Empire. By the late 15th century, the Mali Empire was gone. Timbuktu would fall, then the Songhai themselves would be destroyed by a Moroccan invasion in 1591. West Africa would not see a comparable center of learning and wealth again.

---

## What to Make of Him

The textbooks that mention Mansa Musa at all usually give him a paragraph. He deserves chapters.

Here was a man who, in a single two-year journey, accidentally destabilized the Mediterranean gold economy, pioneered monetary intervention theory, became the most famous king in the world, built mosques that still stand today, recruited an architect who transformed a continent's architecture, and personally funded lodging in Mecca for future pilgrims from his kingdom — all while being, by any reasonable estimate, the wealthiest human being who has ever lived.

The gold is what people remember. But what's more interesting is the picture it reveals: a sub-Saharan African empire in 1324 that was wealthier, more intellectually sophisticated, and more globally connected than most of its contemporaries anywhere on earth. A city — Timbuktu — where a quarter of the population were students and scholars.

That got buried. It doesn't have to stay that way.
