# The Language That Has No Words for Yesterday

*March 22, 2026 — Wildcard (Linguistics)*

In 1977, a young American missionary named Daniel Everett packed up his wife and three small children and moved to a remote stretch of the Maici River in the Brazilian Amazon. His mission was simple: translate the Bible into the language of the Pirahã, a tribe of roughly 300 people who had resisted contact with the outside world for centuries. He expected to give them God. Instead, they took his faith, broke his marriage, and detonated one of the most furious academic wars in modern linguistics.

But let's start with the language itself, because the Pirahã language is, by almost any measure, the strangest on Earth.

## A World Without Numbers

The Pirahã have no words for numbers. Not just "they don't count very high" — they don't count at all. There is no word for "one." No word for "two." No word for "many." They have approximate terms — something like "small amount" and "bigger amount" — but nothing precise. Researchers have tested this rigorously. In one experiment, they placed objects on a table and asked Pirahã speakers to match the quantity. When the numbers were small — one, two, three — they could manage. Beyond that, accuracy collapsed. Not because they're incapable of perceiving quantity, but because their language gives them no tools to encode it.

Think about that for a moment. Every human culture we know of has some system for counting. Even isolated tribes in Papua New Guinea count on body parts. The Pirahã simply... don't.

## No Colors, No Past, No Stories

It gets stranger. The Pirahã have no fixed words for colors. Instead, they describe things comparatively — something is "like blood" or "like the sky" or "like leaves." They have no way to say "red." They can see red perfectly well. They just don't name it.

They have no past tense. Or rather, they have two tense-like markers: one for things happening right now in your immediate experience, and one for things that aren't. But the language doesn't really distinguish between yesterday and a hundred years ago. Both are simply "not now."

They have no creation myths. When Everett asked them where the world came from, they looked at him like the question didn't make sense. Everything has always been the way it is. Why would you think otherwise?

They have no art that outlasts the moment of its making. No collective memory going back more than two generations. No folklore. No fiction. And perhaps most astonishingly — no embedded clauses. No sentences nested inside other sentences. No way to say "The man who was sitting in the canoe caught a fish." Instead, they'd say something like: "The man sat in the canoe. He caught a fish."

This last point — the absence of recursion — is the one that blew up the world of linguistics.

## The War Over Recursion

To understand why, you need to know about Noam Chomsky. For over fifty years, Chomsky had dominated linguistics with a single, powerful idea: that all human languages share a universal grammar, a set of deep structural rules hardwired into our brains by evolution. In 2002, Chomsky and two collaborators published an influential paper arguing that the one feature truly unique to human language — the thing that separates us from every other animal — was recursion. The ability to embed one thought inside another, infinitely. "The dog that chased the cat that ate the mouse that lived in the house that Jack built."

Recursion, they argued, was the engine of human thought. It was what made language language. And it was universal — every human language had it.

Then, in 2005, Daniel Everett — who by then had spent nearly thirty years living with the Pirahã on and off — published a paper called "Cultural Constraints on Grammar and Cognition in Pirahã." His claim was explosive: the Pirahã language has no recursion. None. Not a trace of it.

If Everett was right, then the single feature Chomsky had declared universal and innate — the very foundation of human language — had a counterexample. One small tribe on a muddy river in the Amazon had quietly disproved the most influential theory in modern linguistics.

The reaction was nuclear. Chomsky called Everett a "charlatan." Chomsky's allies accused him of scientific fraud, of fabricating data, of conducting illegal research. They had his permits to do fieldwork in Brazil revoked. Academic journals became battlegrounds. Careers were staked on one side or the other.

Everett didn't flinch. He argued that Pirahã wasn't a deficient language — it was a language perfectly shaped by its culture. The Pirahã don't need recursion because their entire worldview is built on what he called the "immediacy of experience principle." They only talk about things they or someone they know has directly witnessed. No hypotheticals. No distant history. No abstraction for its own sake. Their language isn't missing features. It has exactly the features their life requires.

## The Missionary Who Lost God

And then there's the other story — the personal one. Everett arrived in the Amazon as a devout evangelical Christian. His entire purpose was to bring the Pirahã to Jesus. But the longer he lived with them, the more he noticed something unsettling: they were already happy.

Not happy in the way missionaries imagine "noble savages" to be happy. Happy in a specific, observable, daily way. They laughed constantly. They didn't worry about the future. They didn't grieve the past. They slept in short naps rather than long stretches, waking up to chat and joke throughout the night — which is how Everett's book got its title, *Don't Sleep, There Are Snakes*, the Pirahã's standard "good night" greeting.

When Everett told them about Jesus, they listened politely and then asked if he'd ever met Jesus. No, Everett admitted. Had anyone he knew met Jesus? No. The Pirahã lost interest. In their worldview, information about someone no living person has ever met is not information. It's noise.

By 1982, Everett was having serious doubts about his faith. By 1985, he had privately abandoned it entirely. He didn't tell anyone for over a decade. When he finally did, in the late 1990s, his wife divorced him and two of his three children cut off contact.

A man went to the jungle to convert a tribe, and the tribe converted him — not to any belief system, but to a kind of radical empiricism they had practiced for centuries without ever naming it.

## What the Pirahã Might Be Telling Us

The Pirahã controversy isn't resolved. Chomsky's camp argues that recursion might still be present in Pirahã cognition even if it doesn't show up in their grammar. Everett argues that if a feature isn't in the language, claiming it's hidden in the brain is unfalsifiable — and therefore not science.

But beyond the academic trench warfare, the Pirahã raise a question that's worth sitting with: How much of what we consider fundamental to being human is actually just fundamental to being *us*?

We assume numbers are obvious. Colors need names. The past matters. The future should be planned for. Stories should be told and retold. These feel like features of reality itself. But the Pirahã suggest they might be features of our particular culture — habits so old we've mistaken them for instincts.

Somewhere on the Maici River, about 300 people are living right now with no word for "tomorrow," no interest in where the world came from, and no reason to count past "enough." They whistle their language to each other across the jungle canopy when they hunt. They sleep in short bursts and laugh through the night.

They are, by most accounts, among the happiest people anyone has ever studied.

And they have never once needed the number seven.
