# The City Hidden Beneath a Man's Basement

In 1963, a man in central Turkey decided to renovate his house. He grabbed his tools, knocked down a wall in his basement, and found himself staring into a dark passage that led somewhere he couldn't see. He followed it. The passage opened into a room. That room led to another passage. That passage led to another room. And another. And another.

He had just accidentally discovered Derinkuyu — an entire underground city, eighteen stories deep, carved from solid rock, capable of sheltering 20,000 people. It had been sitting silently beneath his feet, beneath the feet of everyone in his town, for over two thousand years.

## A City That Goes Down, Not Up

Most cities grow outward and upward. Derinkuyu grew downward. Carved into the soft volcanic tuff of Cappadocia — a landscape shaped by ancient eruptions that left behind pillars, cones, and vast plains of rock soft enough to carve with hand tools but hard enough to hold its shape — the city plunges 85 metres (about 280 feet) into the earth. That's roughly the height of a 25-storey building, except inverted, buried, invisible from the surface.

And it isn't just a network of tunnels. It's a functioning city. The complex contains wine and oil presses, stables for livestock, cellars and storage rooms large enough to hold food for thousands, a church with a barrel-vaulted ceiling, schools, living quarters, and communal kitchens. There are even what appear to be small arsenals. On the second floor, archaeologists found a spacious room with a barrel-vaulted ceiling that they believe served as a missionary school with separate study rooms.

Think about what that means. Someone — many someones, across many generations — looked at the rock beneath their homes and decided to carve an entire civilization into it. Not a bunker. Not a basement. A city, with infrastructure, defense systems, and room for tens of thousands of souls.

## Who Built It?

This is where the story gets murky, in the way that ancient history tends to when you go back far enough. The earliest excavations may date to the Hittites, around 2000 BC — the same Bronze Age civilisation that clashed with Ramesses II at the Battle of Kadesh. If that's true, the first pickaxes struck this rock nearly four thousand years ago.

More concrete evidence points to the Phrygians — the people who gave us King Midas of the golden touch legend — expanding the underground spaces in the 8th and 7th centuries BC. They carved the initial tunnels and rooms, laying the skeleton of what would become a much larger complex.

But it was the Byzantine Christians who turned Derinkuyu into a true underground city. From the 7th century onward, as Arab armies swept through Anatolia during the Arab-Byzantine wars, the Christian communities of Cappadocia needed somewhere to hide — not for hours or days, but for weeks and months at a time. They expanded Derinkuyu level by level, floor by floor, until it became one of the largest underground structures ever built.

And remarkably, Cappadocian Greeks were still using these underground cities as late as the 20th century to escape periodic waves of persecution. The last known use was in 1909, when local Christians reportedly hid underground to avoid massacres. A city carved four millennia ago was still saving lives just over a century ago.

## Engineering That Shouldn't Exist

The most staggering thing about Derinkuyu isn't its size. It's the engineering — the kind of engineering you wouldn't expect from people carving rock by hand in an age before electricity, before mechanical ventilation, before structural engineering degrees.

Start with the air. Twenty thousand people underground, with livestock, cooking fires, and oil lamps — that's a ventilation nightmare. Derinkuyu's builders solved it with a system of over 15,000 ventilation shafts, most around 10 centimetres wide, that thread through the rock from the surface down to the deepest levels. Fifty-two of these are massive primary shafts that drive airflow through the entire complex. Some of them double as wells, tapping into underground aquifers so that residents had fresh water even during a siege. The ventilation was so effective that fresh air reached all the way down to the eighth level — 85 metres underground.

Then there are the doors. Massive circular stones, each weighing between 200 and 500 kilograms, were carved to roll into place across tunnel entrances like giant stone wheels. Here's the genius: they could only be operated from the inside. An invader who found a tunnel entrance would face a half-tonne stone disc blocking their path, with no way to push it open. Each floor could be sealed independently, so even if attackers breached one level, the floors above and below could lock down separately. It was, in effect, a layered defense system built entirely from carved rock.

The tunnels themselves were designed with defense in mind. Passages between levels are narrow and low — deliberately so. An invading soldier would have to crouch and move single-file, making it trivially easy for a single defender to hold a corridor. Some tunnels have holes carved into the ceiling above these chokepoints, through which defenders could pour boiling oil or water on anyone trying to force their way through.

## The Tunnel to Nowhere (Or Rather, to Another City)

Perhaps the most astonishing feature of Derinkuyu is what lies beyond it. A tunnel — roughly 9 kilometres long — connects Derinkuyu to Kaymakli, another underground city about 10 kilometres to the north. Kaymakli is smaller, with a capacity for about 3,500 people, but its connection to Derinkuyu suggests something far larger than individual underground shelters: a network. An underground civilization connected by kilometres of hand-carved tunnels through volcanic rock.

And Derinkuyu and Kaymakli aren't alone. Archaeologists have identified over 200 underground settlements across Cappadocia, though only about 40 have more than two levels, and just six have been properly excavated. The region is, quite literally, honeycombed with hidden cities. How many more are down there, still sealed behind someone's basement wall, is anyone's guess.

## The Question That Lingers

Only about 40 metres of Derinkuyu's estimated 85-metre depth have been fully excavated. More than half the city remains unexplored — sealed off, flooded, or simply too unstable to access safely. We are, in a very real sense, still discovering it.

What strikes me most about Derinkuyu isn't the scale, impressive as it is. It's what it says about the people who built it. These were communities who looked at the threat of annihilation and responded not by running, not by fighting, but by building — patiently, across generations, carving a mirror image of their world into the darkness below. They built schools underground. Churches. Wine presses. They didn't just survive down there. They lived.

And then, when the danger passed, they climbed back into the sunlight, sealed the entrances, and went about their lives. The city beneath them was forgotten so completely that it took a man knocking down a wall in his basement — four thousand years later — to remember it was there at all.

Somewhere in Cappadocia, there are probably still cities waiting to be found. All it takes is one curious homeowner with a hammer.
