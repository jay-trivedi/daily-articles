# The Mad Race to Know Pi — And Why It Still Matters

*Happy Pi Day.*

Today is March 14th — 3/14 — and somewhere right now, a server is grinding through trillions of digits of a number we've been chasing for four thousand years. Pi is irrational. It never repeats. It never ends. And yet, century after century, humans keep reaching for the next digit. Why?

The answer involves a self-taught genius from colonial India, two Ukrainian brothers who built a supercomputer in a Manhattan apartment, and a discovery from just three months ago that ties it all back to black holes.

---

## The Ancient Itch

The oldest known attempt to pin down pi comes from a Babylonian clay tablet, dated around 1900 BC. The Babylonians used 3.125 — not bad for a civilization that hadn't invented the concept of zero yet. A few centuries later, an Egyptian scribe named Ahmes wrote on papyrus that pi was roughly 256/81, or about 3.16. Close enough to build a pyramid.

Then came Archimedes, around 250 BC, who had the first genuinely clever idea. Instead of measuring circles, he trapped them. He inscribed a polygon inside a circle and circumscribed another polygon outside it, then kept doubling the number of sides. A 96-sided polygon gave him pi between 3.1408 and 3.1429. It was the first algorithm — a method you could repeat to get ever-closer answers.

Two centuries later, Chinese mathematician Zu Chongzhi pushed the same basic approach to a staggering level of precision: 355/113, accurate to seven digits. This approximation was so good that it wouldn't be surpassed for nearly a thousand years.

## Madhava's Infinite Leap

Around the 14th century, something extraordinary happened in Kerala, India. A mathematician named Madhava of Sangamagrama discovered that pi could be expressed as an infinite series — an endless sum of fractions that, term by term, converges on the exact value. This was the first *exact* formula for pi, predating European calculus by roughly 250 years.

Madhava's work launched a tradition. In the centuries that followed, mathematicians discovered dozens of infinite series for pi, each one a different path to the same unreachable destination. John Wallis found an infinite product in 1655. Leibniz and Gregory independently found another series. Each formula was a philosophical statement as much as a mathematical one: pi is knowable, but only in the limit. You can get as close as you want. You can never arrive.

## The Man Who Knew Infinity

Then came Srinivasa Ramanujan.

In 1914, a 26-year-old clerk from Madras published a paper containing 17 formulas for computing pi. They were unlike anything mathematicians had seen before. Where previous series might yield one or two correct digits per term, Ramanujan's formulas delivered *eight*. They converged on pi with ferocious speed, as if they were taking a shortcut through some hidden mathematical dimension.

Ramanujan had almost no formal training. He'd taught himself from a single borrowed textbook. He claimed his formulas came to him in dreams, placed there by the goddess Namagiri. His Cambridge collaborator G.H. Hardy — one of the greatest mathematicians of the 20th century — said that Ramanujan's results "must be true, because, if they were not true, no one would have the imagination to invent them."

For decades, Ramanujan's pi formulas were admired but mostly theoretical curiosities. Computers didn't exist yet, and nobody needed eight digits of pi per term when you were computing by hand. But when the digital age arrived, everything changed.

## A Supercomputer in a Living Room

In 1988, two brothers from Kyiv — David and Gregory Chudnovsky — took one of Ramanujan's formulas and turbocharged it. Their algorithm, now known as the Chudnovsky algorithm, produces roughly 14 correct digits of pi per term. It is, in essence, Ramanujan's insight refined into a computational weapon.

But the brothers didn't stop at the formula. They needed a machine to run it on, and they couldn't afford a Cray supercomputer. So they built one. In Gregory's apartment in Manhattan.

They called it *m-zero*. It occupied the former living room, with cables and circuit boards snaking into adjacent rooms. At the time, the world's most powerful supercomputers included the Cray Y-MP, the Thinking Machines CM-5, various Hitachi and Fujitsu machines — and Gregory Chudnovsky's apartment. Their homemade rig was roughly as powerful as a commercial Cray, assembled from mail-order parts.

Using *m-zero* and their algorithm, the Chudnovsky brothers computed pi to over two billion digits in the early 1990s, smashing existing records. The achievement was absurd, heroic, and deeply human — two brothers driven not by any practical need but by the sheer desire to *know*.

## The Trillion-Digit Era

The Chudnovsky algorithm has dominated pi computation ever since. Every major world record in the last three decades has used it:

- **2009**: 2.7 trillion digits
- **2020**: 50 trillion digits
- **2022**: Google Cloud hit 100 trillion digits using massive cloud infrastructure
- **2024**: 202 trillion digits

And then, in November 2025, StorageReview shattered the record again: **314 trillion digits of pi** — a number that feels almost too perfect, as if pi itself chose the milestone. They did it on a *single server*: a Dell PowerEdge R7725 with dual 192-core AMD EPYC processors and forty solid-state drives, running for 110 days straight. The entire computation consumed about 4,300 kilowatt-hours — roughly what an American household uses in four months.

Three hundred and fourteen trillion digits. Written out, the number would stretch past the observable universe. And every single one of those digits was computed using the formula that two brothers derived from a dream that a clerk in Madras had in 1914.

## The Punchline Nobody Expected

Here's where the story takes its strangest turn.

In December 2025, physicists at the Indian Institute of Science published a paper in *Physical Review Letters* that sent ripples through both the math and physics communities. They discovered that Ramanujan's pi formulas — the same ones powering trillion-digit computations — appear naturally within the mathematics of modern physics.

Specifically, the formulas emerge in logarithmic conformal field theories, which describe systems with scale invariance — systems that look the same no matter how far you zoom in, like fractals, like the turbulent swirl of a river, like the event horizon of a black hole. The mathematical structures Ramanujan intuited a century ago, seemingly out of nowhere, turn out to be woven into the fabric of how the universe behaves at its most fundamental level.

Ramanujan didn't know about conformal field theory. He didn't know about black holes — the term wouldn't be coined for another 50 years. He was working with a borrowed textbook and a slate, in a small town in southern India. And yet, his formulas anticipated structures that physicists are only now beginning to understand.

## Why We Keep Counting

Nobody needs 314 trillion digits of pi. NASA uses 15 digits to navigate spacecraft across the solar system. Forty digits would be enough to compute the circumference of the observable universe to within the width of a hydrogen atom. Everything beyond that is, in a strictly practical sense, useless.

But that misses the point entirely. The race for digits has never been about pi. It's been about us — our tools, our algorithms, our hardware, our stubbornness. Each record is a benchmark of human capability dressed up as a number.

And sometimes, as Ramanujan showed, the pursuit of something "useless" turns out to illuminate the deepest structures of reality. The digits may be infinite and the destination unreachable, but the journey keeps revealing things we never expected to find.

That's worth celebrating. Happy Pi Day.
