# The Glowing Orb That Physics Can't Explain

*March 17, 2026 — Wildcard*

In 1638, during a violent thunderstorm in the tiny Devon village of Widecombe-in-the-Moor, something impossible walked into a church. Witnesses described an eight-foot ball of fire that smashed through the stone wall, tore through the congregation, killed four people, and injured sixty others. The villagers, being good 17th-century Englishmen, assumed it was the Devil himself.

Nearly four hundred years later, we've ruled out Satan. But we haven't replaced him with a better explanation.

Welcome to ball lightning — one of the last great unsolved mysteries in all of physics.

## Thousands of Witnesses, Zero Consensus

Here's what makes ball lightning so maddening: it's not some obscure theoretical prediction that no one's seen. Over 2,000 credible reports have been filed over the past three centuries. The descriptions are remarkably consistent. People report glowing spheres, usually between the size of a golf ball and a beach ball, appearing during or just after thunderstorms. They're typically yellowish to reddish, though blue, green, and white have all been reported. They drift lazily through the air — sometimes horizontally, sometimes hovering — for anywhere from a few seconds to a full minute. Then they vanish, either quietly fading out or exploding with a sharp bang.

The really strange part? Multiple witnesses have reported ball lightning *passing through solid walls and windows* without leaving a mark. Others have seen it drift down the aisle of an airplane at cruising altitude. In 1963, an electronics researcher aboard Eastern Airlines Flight 539 watched a glowing sphere about eight inches across emerge from the cockpit, float calmly down the aisle of the all-metal aircraft, and disappear near the rear of the plane. Nobody was hurt. Nobody could explain it.

For centuries, mainstream science dismissed the whole thing as folklore. Lightning makes people see weird stuff, the thinking went. Case closed. Ball lightning sat in the same bin as UFOs and Bigfoot — something "serious" researchers didn't touch.

That changed, slowly, as too many credible observers — pilots, scientists, engineers — kept reporting the same thing.

## The Monk, the Physicist, and the Accident

The written record goes back further than most people realize. The earliest known English account was penned by Gervase, a monk at Canterbury's Christ Church Cathedral, sometime between 1180 and 1199. His description — a luminous globe appearing during a storm — predates the next credible English report by nearly 450 years.

By the 19th century, the phenomenon had caught the attention of serious minds. Reports were being filed with the French Academy of Sciences, complete with sworn statements. But it was Nikola Tesla, working in his legendarily unhinged Colorado Springs laboratory in 1899, who first claimed to have cracked the code. "I succeeded in determining the mode of their formation and producing them artificially," Tesla wrote. Inside his wooden lab, powered by a monstrous coil 52 feet across that hurled millions of volts through the air beneath a 142-foot pole topped with a copper ball, Tesla reportedly produced small fireballs about 1.5 inches across. Ball lightning, he implied, was just another electrical phenomenon waiting to be tamed.

But Tesla's notes were characteristically cryptic, and nobody could reliably reproduce his results for over a century. It wasn't until two Ohio engineers, Kenneth and James Corum, revisited Tesla's notebooks that anyone managed to produce artificial ball lightning to order — and even then, the laboratory versions behaved differently from the wild ones.

## The Lucky Break in Lanzhou

Then, in 2012, something extraordinary happened purely by accident.

A team from Northwest Normal University in Lanzhou, China, was out on the Tibetan Plateau studying ordinary cloud-to-ground lightning. They had video cameras and spectrometers — instruments that break light into its component wavelengths, essentially reading the chemical fingerprint of anything that glows. They were expecting to record normal lightning strikes.

Instead, their cameras captured 1.64 seconds of what appeared to be genuine ball lightning, forming right after a bolt hit the ground and drifting horizontally for about ten meters before fading out. A high-speed camera running at 3,000 frames per second caught the final 0.78 seconds.

But the real prize was the spectrometer data. When the researchers analyzed the light from the ball, they found emission lines of silicon, calcium, iron, nitrogen, and oxygen — elements found in soil, not in air. The parent lightning bolt, by contrast, showed mainly ionized nitrogen, which is what you'd expect from superheated atmosphere.

This was the first spectral fingerprint of natural ball lightning ever recorded. And it pointed toward a theory that had been kicking around for years: that when lightning strikes the ground, it vaporizes the silicon and other minerals in the soil, and these hot particles somehow self-organize into a glowing, floating ball.

Published in January 2014, the Lanzhou data was a genuine breakthrough. But "pointing toward a theory" and "explaining the phenomenon" are very different things.

## Five Theories, No Winner

The silicon-soil theory explains why ball lightning often appears right after a ground strike, and it matches the spectral data from Lanzhou beautifully. But it can't explain reports of ball lightning forming indoors, passing through walls, appearing inside sealed aircraft, or hovering over water.

So the theories keep multiplying:

**The plasma vortex model** suggests that a lightning strike can create a spinning ring of hot plasma — essentially a tiny, self-contained tornado of ionized gas — that traps energy and glows as it slowly dissipates. It's elegant physics, but plasma should cool and disperse in milliseconds, not drift around for thirty seconds.

**The aerosol battery theory**, proposed by Russian physicist Oleg Meshcheryakov, imagines ball lightning as a cloud of composite nanoparticles, each one essentially a tiny battery. Surface discharges between particles sustain the glow. This model claims to explain nearly all observed properties, including wall penetration, but it's fiendishly difficult to test.

**The microwave cavity hypothesis** proposes that ball lightning is atmospheric gas trapped in a self-generated electromagnetic standing wave — essentially, the ball is its own microwave oven. This could explain the long duration and the lack of heat felt by nearby observers.

**The hallucination theory** — yes, this is a real scientific proposal — suggests that the intense electromagnetic fields near a lightning strike can stimulate the visual cortex, making witnesses "see" a glowing ball that isn't physically there. This would explain the wall-penetration reports (hard to be stopped by a wall if you don't exist), but it struggles with cases where multiple observers see the same ball from different angles, or where physical damage is left behind.

Each theory explains some observations and fails on others. No single model accounts for the full range of reported behavior.

## Why It Matters

You might wonder why physicists care so much about a rare atmospheric oddity. The answer is that ball lightning sits right at the intersection of plasma physics, atmospheric chemistry, and electromagnetic theory — three fields that matter enormously for everything from fusion energy to weather prediction to telecommunications. If we can't explain a glowing ball that thousands of people have seen, what else are we missing about how energy behaves in the atmosphere?

There's also a deeper point. We live in an era where we've photographed black holes, detected gravitational waves from colliding neutron stars, and mapped the cosmic microwave background to exquisite precision. We have sent robots to Mars and sequenced the human genome. And yet a luminous ball the size of a grapefruit, floating through someone's living room during a thunderstorm, remains beyond our understanding.

Ball lightning is a humbling reminder that nature still has tricks we haven't figured out. The universe is not done surprising us. Somewhere right now, during a storm you'll never hear about, a small glowing sphere is drifting silently through the rain, carrying a secret that the smartest people on the planet still can't crack.

And honestly? That's kind of wonderful.
