# The City That Danced Itself to Death

On a sweltering July morning in 1518, a woman named Frau Troffea stepped out of her half-timbered house onto a narrow cobblestone street in Strasbourg and began to dance. There was no music. No festival. No celebration. She simply started moving — twisting, twirling, shaking — and she did not stop.

Her husband watched. Neighbours watched. Hours passed. Night fell. She kept dancing. By the next morning, her feet were swollen and bleeding, and still she danced. For somewhere between four and six days straight, Frau Troffea moved without rest, without food, without any apparent ability to stop herself.

And then it got worse. Because other people started joining in.

## The Contagion Spreads

Within a week, more than 30 people were dancing in the streets of Strasbourg. By August, the number had swelled to somewhere between 50 and 400 — the historical records vary, but even the conservative estimate is staggering. These were not revellers. Witnesses described their movements as spasmodic and convulsive, their bodies drenched in sweat, their faces contorted in expressions that looked nothing like joy. Some wept as they danced. Some begged for it to stop.

And some of them died. From heart attacks. From strokes. From sheer exhaustion. The exact death toll is lost to history, but contemporary accounts confirm that people were collapsing in the streets of Strasbourg, killed by the simple act of involuntary movement.

The city council was baffled. This was the Holy Roman Empire in the early sixteenth century — a world without germ theory, without psychology, without any framework for understanding what was happening. So they did what seemed logical at the time: they consulted physicians. The physicians, working from the dominant medical theory of the era (that illness was caused by imbalances in the body's "humours"), concluded that the dancers had "overheated blood" and prescribed a treatment that, in hindsight, reads like satire.

They told the dancers to keep dancing.

## The Prescription That Made It Worse

The reasoning went like this: if the dancers' blood was hot, the cure was to let them dance it out of their systems. So the Strasbourg authorities went all in. They cleared two guild halls and a grain market to give the dancers more room. They hired musicians — actual professional musicians — to play accompaniment. They even brought in strong young men whose job was to hold the dancers upright when they started to collapse, so they could keep going.

It was, to put it bluntly, the worst possible response. Instead of containing the outbreak, Strasbourg had essentially built a dance floor and hired a band. The epidemic exploded. More and more people were swept into the mania. The city's plan was like trying to put out a fire by throwing kindling on it.

It took weeks for the authorities to reverse course. Eventually, they banned public dancing entirely, closed the dance halls, and — crucially — carted the afflicted to a mountaintop shrine dedicated to Saint Vitus. There, the dancers were given small crosses, led around an altar in red shoes, and sprinkled with holy water. And then, gradually, over the course of early September 1518, the dancing stopped.

The whole episode had lasted roughly two months.

## So What Actually Happened?

This is the question that has haunted historians and medical researchers for five centuries. The Dancing Plague of 1518 is not legend or folklore — it is one of the best-documented mass events of the medieval period. Notes from Strasbourg's city council survive. Physician records survive. Sermons referencing the dancers survive. Multiple independent chroniclers wrote about it. This happened.

The most commonly floated explanation is **ergot poisoning** — contamination of grain by the ergot fungus, which produces chemicals structurally related to LSD. The idea is appealing on the surface: hallucinogenic bread making people act strangely. But it doesn't hold up well under scrutiny. Ergot poisoning causes violent muscle contractions and restricted blood flow to the extremities, often leading to gangrene. It would be essentially impossible to dance for days on end while poisoned by ergot. And it doesn't explain why nearly every recorded outbreak of choreomania — and there were at least seven others across medieval Europe — occurred along the Rhine and Moselle rivers, regions with very different crops and climates.

The most compelling explanation comes from historian **John Waller**, who spent years in the Strasbourg archives piecing together the context. His argument is that the Dancing Plague was a case of **mass psychogenic illness** — a phenomenon where extreme psychological stress manifests as physical symptoms that spread through a community via a kind of social contagion.

And in 1518, the people of Strasbourg were under extraordinary stress. In the years immediately preceding the outbreak, the region had been hammered by a series of catastrophes: severe famines, an unusually brutal winter, outbreaks of smallpox and syphilis, and political instability as the old feudal order crumbled. People were starving, sick, and terrified. Apocalyptic preachers roamed the streets. Heavenly portents — including the Great Comet of 1492, still fresh in living memory — had convinced many that the end times were near.

## The Saint Who Made Them Dance

Here's where it gets really interesting. Waller discovered that in the folk belief of the Rhine region, there was a specific supernatural figure who was believed to have the power to curse people with compulsive dancing: **Saint Vitus**. By the fifteenth century, an established tradition held that if you angered Saint Vitus — through sin, impiety, or simply bad luck — he could strike you with an uncontrollable urge to dance. They even had a name for it: *Veitstanz*, Saint Vitus' Dance.

Waller's argument is elegant: in a population pushed to the edge of psychological collapse by famine, disease, and existential terror, the culturally available template for breakdown was *dancing*. If you lived in sixteenth-century Strasbourg and your mind snapped under the weight of starvation and plague, your culture told you what form that breakdown would take. Saint Vitus would curse you, and you would dance.

This also explains why the cure worked. When the dancers were taken to the shrine of Saint Vitus and given the prescribed ritual — red shoes, holy water, wooden crosses — they were essentially being given permission to stop. The curse had been lifted. The saint had been appeased. In the framework of their own belief system, they were free.

## Not the First, Not the Last

The Strasbourg outbreak was the most dramatic, but it wasn't unique. The earliest recorded dancing plague struck in the seventh century. One of the largest erupted in 1374, sweeping through towns along the Rhine — Aachen, Cologne, Metz — with thousands of people reportedly affected. Smaller outbreaks popped up throughout the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries.

And then they stopped. After the Reformation reshaped European religion and the Enlightenment began to reshape European thought, the dancing plagues vanished. As the cultural template changed — as people stopped believing that saints could curse them with compulsive movement — the specific form of the illness disappeared with it.

Which raises a genuinely unsettling question: mass psychogenic illness hasn't gone away. It still happens. But its form changes with the culture. In the medieval Rhine valley, extreme stress made people dance. In other times and places, it has produced mass fainting, uncontrollable laughter, mysterious rashes, and — some researchers argue — certain modern phenomena that we are still reluctant to categorise this way.

## The Mind Is Stranger Than We Think

The Dancing Plague of 1518 is not just a weird historical footnote. It's a window into something fundamental about human psychology: that the mind can hijack the body in ways that are terrifyingly literal, and that the *form* that hijacking takes is shaped by what a culture believes is possible.

Frau Troffea didn't choose to dance. Neither did the hundreds who followed her. Their bodies moved against their will, driven by a kind of suffering so deep that it bypassed conscious control entirely and expressed itself as pure, involuntary motion.

Five hundred years later, we still don't fully understand how that works. We have better names for it now — psychogenic, psychosomatic, conversion disorder — but the mechanism remains stubbornly mysterious. The brain decides the body is going to do something, and the body does it, and the conscious mind is just along for the ride.

Strasbourg's dancers were trying to tell us something. We're still listening.
