# The Hole the Soviets Drilled Into Hell

On a windswept stretch of the Kola Peninsula, a hundred and fifty miles north of the Arctic Circle and walking distance from the Norwegian border, there is a rusted metal cap bolted into a concrete slab. The cap is about twenty-three centimeters across, roughly the size of a dinner plate. Beneath it, a hole plunges more than twelve kilometers straight down into the Earth. That hole is deeper than Mount Everest is tall. It is deeper than the Mariana Trench. For thirty-one years, from 1989 until it was surpassed by an oil well in Qatar in 2008, it was the deepest artificial point on the planet — and by "true depth," straight down rather than angled, it was still the record until 2011.

The Soviets drilled it. They did it for science, they did it for prestige, and they did it because the Americans had tried the same thing and failed. Along the way, they found water where no water should have been, fossils where no life should have lived, and rock that moved like plastic under their drill bits. They recorded nothing screaming. Despite what the tabloids said.

## The race beneath the crust

In the early 1960s, two superpowers were racing one another in every direction they could think of — upward into orbit, sideways into new ideologies, and increasingly, downward. In 1958, American oceanographer Walter Munk floated a proposal called Project Mohole. The idea was to drill through the ocean floor — which is much thinner than continental crust — and puncture the Mohorovičić discontinuity, the mysterious boundary between Earth's crust and its mantle. Nobody had ever sampled mantle rock. Everything we thought we knew about the inside of the planet came from indirect evidence: seismic waves, magnetic readings, the behavior of volcanoes.

Mohole drilled five holes off the coast of Guadalupe in 1961. It reached 183 meters into the seafloor. Then Congress discovered the budget had ballooned from $5 million to $127 million, and in 1966 the project was killed.

The Soviets, watching from across the Cold War, decided to try a different approach. Instead of thin ocean crust, they would drill into thick continental crust — but they would drill a hole so deep, and so precise, that it would teach them more about the inside of the Earth than the Americans could have learned from a shallow puncture of the mantle.

On May 24, 1970 — the hundredth anniversary of Lenin's birth — the drill began turning at a site called SG-3, on the Kola Peninsula. The surface drilling rig looked like an oil derrick, but the machinery inside was something else entirely. The drill bit at the end of the string didn't spin the way a normal drill does. Instead, the bit was fed mud under enormous pressure, and the mud turned a turbine that spun only the bit itself. The drill pipe stayed still, which prevented it from tearing itself apart under its own weight.

Even so, the pipe was doing something no pipe had ever done. At twelve kilometers long, hanging from a derrick in Arctic Russia, it weighed nearly 200 tons. Every time they had to pull it out to change the bit, it took eighteen hours to come up. Then eighteen hours to go back down.

## What they found

By 1979, the Kola hole had passed the previous depth record — an oil well in Oklahoma called the Bertha Rogers hole, 9,583 meters deep. By 1989, it had reached 12,262 meters. That's where it stopped. Not because the Soviets gave up, but because the Earth itself started fighting back in a way nobody had predicted.

Three findings from that hole rewrote textbooks.

**The mystery of the missing basalt layer.** Geologists had long believed that continental crust was layered like a cake: granite on top, then basalt underneath, then the mantle. Seismic studies had shown what looked like a "basalt boundary" at around 7 kilometers, where shock waves suddenly moved faster. When Kola punched through that boundary, there was no basalt. It was all granite — fractured, metamorphosed granite — and the reason shock waves sped up was that the rock was saturated with water. The "boundary" wasn't a change in composition. It was a change in how the rock held water.

**The water that shouldn't exist.** At six kilometers down, in rock dating back 2.7 billion years, the drillers found free-flowing water. Not aquifer water seeping in from above — this was water locked inside the rock, squeezed out of mineral crystals by unimaginable pressure. The standard theory said water couldn't exist at those depths. It was supposed to be bound into the chemistry of the minerals. Finding it sloshing around the borehole, hot and saline, forced geochemists to rewrite their models of how the deep crust works.

**Microfossils from a forgotten ocean.** Perhaps the most astonishing result came from rock core pulled up from between six and seven kilometers down. Soviet paleontologist Yevgeny Rudakov found twenty-four distinct species of single-celled microfossils — plankton shells — preserved in that rock. They were two billion years old. They showed that life had existed in the oceans of the early Earth, and that an ocean had once covered what is now the Kola Peninsula, and that those organisms had then been buried alive, slowly, over unimaginable spans of geological time, until a Soviet drill bit yanked them back into the sunlight.

## The wall of heat

So why did the drill stop at 12,262 meters?

Standard geological models had predicted that the temperature at that depth would be around 100°C. It was 180°C. That's hot enough that the rock itself began to deform. The drillers reported that the deeper hole felt like it was drilling through "plastic" — the granite, under enormous pressure and heat, flowed. Every time they pulled the drill out, the walls of the hole would start to close around the empty space where the pipe had been.

Drill bits stopped lasting. Sections of pipe got stuck and had to be abandoned. They ended up with multiple "sidetrack" holes branching from the main shaft, because the main shaft kept getting jammed with broken equipment they couldn't retrieve.

They kept trying through the early 1990s, but then the Soviet Union collapsed, and with it the funding. By 1995, the research facility was mostly closed. The borehole itself was sealed — not for mystical reasons, but because an open twelve-kilometer pipe to the interior of the Earth is, at minimum, a workplace hazard. The buildings above it were demolished for scrap in 2008.

## The hoax, because of course there was a hoax

In 1989, a Finnish newspaper — reportedly as an April Fool's joke — ran a story claiming the Soviet drillers had lowered a microphone into the hole and recorded the screams of the damned rising from hell. The story was picked up by an American Christian broadcast network, which ran it without irony. Audio was fabricated. Over the decades, the "Well to Hell" tape has circulated across radio shows, chain emails, and now TikTok, despite the fact that no such microphone was ever lowered, and that the actual researchers — who were real people, with real publications, many of whom are still alive — have repeatedly debunked it.

The real story didn't need demons. A Soviet drill, in the cold of Murmansk, quietly pulled two-billion-year-old plankton out of rock that was supposed to be dead. That should be enough.

## What the hole left behind

The Kola Superdeep Borehole got less than a third of a percent of the way to the center of the Earth. The planet is 6,371 kilometers to its core. Kola made it to 12.262 kilometers. And it stopped because the rock started flowing.

There is something almost theological about that result. We can build spacecraft that reach interstellar space. We have photographed the surface of Pluto. We have mapped the human genome and simulated the first second after the Big Bang. But a hole in the ground, in the direction of the planet we stand on, we cannot push past thirteen kilometers. Not yet. Maybe not ever with current technology. The deep Earth is more alien to us than Mars.

The cap on that hole in Kola is welded shut now. But if you stood on it, you could tell yourself the truth: beneath your feet is the single longest line any human has ever drawn straight down, and it ends in a place where rock behaves like honey, at a temperature hot enough to bake bread, saturated with water that has been trapped there since before animals existed.

We call it the deepest hole. It's also the longest question mark.
