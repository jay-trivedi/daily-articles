# The Woman Who Found the Law Beneath Physics

In 1915, two of the most famous mathematicians alive — David Hilbert and Felix Klein — wrote to Albert Einstein with a problem. His new theory of general relativity had a strange wrinkle in it. Energy did not seem to be conserved the way it was in the old Newtonian world. Something was off in the bookkeeping of the universe, and they could not see where the money was going.

So they invited a thirty-three-year-old German woman named Emmy Noether to Göttingen to help them fix it.

She fixed it. And in doing so, she wrote down a result so deep that every physicist working today — every quantum mechanic, every string theorist, every engineer simulating a satellite's orbit — stands on top of it. Her theorem is the silent scaffolding beneath modern physics. And yet, when she died twenty years later, most of the world had never heard her name.

## Going to school through a side door

Amalie "Emmy" Noether was born in the Bavarian town of Erlangen on March 23, 1882. Her father Max was a respected mathematician at the local university. Her younger brother Fritz would become one too. Emmy, who was a girl, was not supposed to.

German universities in the 1890s did not admit women. When Emmy decided she wanted to study math, she had to ask each individual professor for permission to sit in on his lectures. Of the 986 students at the University of Erlangen in 1900, two were women. She was one of them.

She finished her dissertation in 1907 — a thorny piece of work on invariants of ternary biquadratic forms that she would later dismiss as "crap." For the next seven years, she taught at Erlangen for no pay, often covering her ailing father's classes. She published paper after paper in a field — abstract algebra — that barely existed yet.

Then in 1915, Hilbert's letter came.

## The Göttingen fight

Göttingen in 1915 was the mathematical center of the world. Hilbert was its king. He read Noether's papers and decided, without ceremony, that she belonged there.

The faculty disagreed. Some professors, particularly in the humanities, were horrified. What would the soldiers returning from the trenches think, they asked, when they came back to their university to be taught by a woman? Hilbert listened to this and then stood up and delivered the line that is still quoted a century later:

"I do not see that the sex of the candidate is an argument against her admission as Privatdozent. After all, we are a university, not a bathhouse."

The faculty was unmoved. For four years Emmy Noether lectured at Göttingen under David Hilbert's name — his courses on the official schedule, taught by her. It was not until 1919 that she was finally allowed her Habilitation, the right to lecture under her own name. She would never be given a full professorship in Germany. Her eventual title in 1923, "unofficial associate professor," came with a small stipend. Some years she was paid nothing at all.

## The theorem

The problem Hilbert had called her for was this. In Einstein's general relativity, spacetime itself bends and warps. If space is no longer a fixed background, what does it even mean to say energy is conserved? Where is the ledger?

Noether's solution, published in 1918 in a paper titled *Invariante Variationsprobleme*, reframed the entire question. Conservation, she showed, is not a separate law tacked on to physics. It is a consequence of symmetry.

Here is what she proved, in a sentence: **every continuous symmetry of the laws of nature gives you a conserved quantity, and every conserved quantity comes from a symmetry.**

That sounds abstract until you list what it actually means.

- The laws of physics don't change from one moment to the next — time is symmetric. That symmetry is why **energy** is conserved.
- The laws of physics don't change from one place to another — space is symmetric. That is why **momentum** is conserved.
- The laws of physics don't care which direction you're facing — rotations are symmetric. That is why **angular momentum** is conserved.

Every conservation law you learned in school is, secretly, a fingerprint left by a symmetry. Noether's theorem welded two of the oldest ideas in physics — *things stay the same* and *some quantities can never be destroyed* — into one principle. They were the same principle all along.

The implications ran in every direction. In the 20th century, when physicists went looking for new forces, they did it by proposing new symmetries and using Noether's theorem to work out what should be conserved. The entire Standard Model of particle physics — quarks, electrons, the Higgs boson — is built by postulating symmetries and then letting Noether translate them into conserved charges. When CERN confirmed the Higgs in 2012, they were, in a real sense, confirming a symmetry that Emmy Noether had taught physics how to look for.

Einstein, in a 1935 letter to the New York Times, called her "the most significant creative mathematical genius thus far produced since the higher education of women began."

## The other half of her life

Noether's theorem would have been enough for any mathematician's career. She was just getting started.

Over the next decade and a half, she essentially reinvented algebra. The version of algebra you find in textbooks today — rings, ideals, modules, chains of subgroups climbing toward structure — is not Galois's or Gauss's. It is hers. She taught her students, who came to be called the "Noether boys," to stop thinking about numbers and start thinking about the relationships between operations. What mattered was not *what* you were adding or multiplying, but the *shape* of the rules that governed it.

Her seminars were famously chaotic. She paced, she shouted, she argued with herself. Her hair came loose. She waved her arms. Students adored her. One of them, Bartel van der Waerden, wrote the textbook *Moderne Algebra* in 1930 based largely on her lectures. It is the book that taught the 20th century how to do algebra.

## Exile

In April 1933, the Nazi regime passed the "Law for the Restoration of the Professional Civil Service." It fired Jews from university posts. Noether, Jewish, was dismissed from Göttingen.

For a while she kept meeting her students in her apartment, sometimes with young men in brown SA uniforms sitting in her living room — they were her students too, and she refused to turn them away. Eventually she accepted a one-year guest professorship at Bryn Mawr, a women's college in Pennsylvania. She was, for the first time in her life, a paid full professor.

She thrived. She gave weekly lectures at the Institute for Advanced Study in Princeton — Einstein was there, so was Weyl, so was von Neumann. Colleagues described her as radiantly happy.

Eighteen months after she arrived in America, a large ovarian cyst was discovered. She went in for surgery in April 1935. The operation went well. Four days later, she developed a sudden high fever and died. She was fifty-three.

Weyl's eulogy said that she had been "a great mathematician, the greatest, I firmly believe, her sex has ever produced, and a great woman." A hundred years on, we can drop a couple of qualifiers from that sentence.

## What she saw

There is something almost unbearable about what Noether saw. Physics, before her, was a collection of observed laws — this quantity is conserved, that one is not, here is a formula for momentum, here is one for energy — each a fact about the universe that had to be memorized separately, like a list of capital cities.

She showed they weren't a list at all. They were a single sentence written in a different language.

Every time something stays the same in the universe — every time a planet keeps its orbit, every time a bullet carries its momentum, every time a photon leaves a distant galaxy with the same energy it was born with — it is because somewhere, at a deeper level, something *else* did not change. The universe is conservative because it is repetitive. It repeats because it is symmetric.

A woman who was not allowed to be a professor found the reason the world has shape. That is the joke at the center of physics, and it is also, quietly, its most beautiful theorem.
