# The Failed Student Who Thought He Was Jesus's Brother — and Killed Twenty Million People

In the summer of 1837, a young man named Hong Xiuquan walked out of an examination hall in the southern Chinese city of Canton for the third time, having failed. He was a village schoolteacher, the son of a poor Hakka farmer, and the imperial civil service exam was his family's one road out of obscurity. Three failures was humiliating. A fourth would come in 1843. That one would break him — and, in breaking him, set in motion the deadliest civil war in human history.

If you have never heard of the Taiping Rebellion, you are not alone. Most people haven't. And yet the war that Hong Xiuquan launched killed somewhere between twenty and thirty million people between 1850 and 1864. For comparison: the entire American Civil War, fought during almost exactly the same years, killed around 750,000. World War I killed about 20 million. The Taiping Rebellion is one of those facts that, once you know it, makes you wonder what else got pushed off the syllabus.

## The Fever Dream

After his third failure, Hong fell sick with a fever that lasted forty days. He hallucinated. He was taken up to a celestial court. An old man with a golden beard handed him a sword and told him to purge the demons from the world. A middle-aged man called him "younger brother" and told him his true family was in heaven.

When the fever passed, Hong went back to teaching village children and thought very little of any of this. He tried the exam a fourth time. He failed again.

Then, in 1843, his cousin handed him a Christian missionary tract — a pamphlet written in classical Chinese by a Protestant convert named Liang Fa, distributed outside the exam halls to failed candidates who were presumably in a receptive mood. Hong read it. And suddenly the fever dream snapped into focus.

The old man with the golden beard was God. The middle-aged man was Jesus. And Hong Xiuquan, failed schoolteacher from Guangdong, was Jesus's younger brother, sent to cleanse the world of demons. The demons, he concluded, were the Manchu rulers of the Qing dynasty, who had conquered Han China two hundred years earlier and still sat on the dragon throne in Beijing.

## The Heavenly Kingdom of Great Peace

Hong began preaching. He smashed Confucian tablets in schoolrooms. He gathered followers among the Hakka, a marginalized minority who shared his poverty and his grievance. By 1850 he had a movement — the God Worshipping Society — and when a local magistrate tried to arrest them, the movement became an army.

Hong called his new state the Taiping Tianguo — the Heavenly Kingdom of Great Peace. Its ideology was a strange, volatile fusion: fundamentalist Christianity (with Hong himself as a sort of third person of a reworked trinity), Han nationalism against the Manchu, and a radical social program that was centuries ahead of its time. The Taipings abolished private property. They banned foot binding. They banned opium, alcohol, and tobacco. They gave women positions in the army and the bureaucracy. They proclaimed equal land distribution.

They were also, by any reasonable measure, a millenarian death cult. Men and women were kept in strictly segregated camps. Adultery was punished by beheading. Anyone caught with opium was executed. The Taipings swept north through the Yangtze valley in a tide of millions, burning Confucian temples and Buddhist monasteries and killing anyone who resisted.

In March 1853 they took Nanjing, the old southern capital of China, after a three-week siege. Hong moved into a former Ming palace, renamed the city Tianjing — Heavenly Capital — and essentially disappeared inside it. For the next eleven years, as the war consumed the Chinese heartland, the Heavenly King almost never left his palace. He wrote religious poetry. He expanded his harem to around eighty consorts. He issued increasingly cryptic decrees. The actual running of the war was handed off to subordinate kings, who promptly started murdering each other.

## The Scale Is the Point

To understand why this war killed so many, you have to understand the terrain. The Taipings held a huge stretch of the Yangtze basin — some of the most densely populated agricultural land on earth, the rice bowl that fed perhaps 150 million people. The fighting raged across it for fourteen years. Armies on both sides lived off the countryside. When they couldn't, they burned it so the other side couldn't either.

Famine followed the armies. Then disease followed the famine. Then refugees fled the disease into cities, where they starved in turn. Imperial reprisals were genocidal: when the Qing retook a rebel city, they often executed the entire adult male population. The Taipings, for their part, massacred Manchu civilians — in the 1853 fall of Nanjing, they killed every Manchu man, woman, and child they could find, perhaps 40,000 people.

The deadliest year was probably 1862. Anhui province alone may have lost half its population. Some counties in Jiangsu went from a million people to under a hundred thousand. Travelers described rice paddies overrun by forest, villages inhabited only by dogs, rivers so full of corpses the boatmen complained they slowed their progress. The demographer Ping-ti Ho, working from Qing census records, estimated that the lower Yangtze lost more than 70 million people — some killed, most displaced, dead of hunger and cholera in places no one counted.

## The Ever-Victorious Army

The Qing dynasty's own army — the Manchu banner forces — proved almost useless. What eventually turned the war were two things. First, a brilliant Confucian scholar-general named Zeng Guofan, who raised a new, locally recruited army in Hunan province and slowly, methodically, strangled the rebel heartland. Second, the British.

This is the part that always surprises. An American mercenary named Frederick Townsend Ward, and after his death a British officer named Charles Gordon, commanded a mercenary force called the Ever-Victorious Army, paid for by Shanghai merchants who were terrified the Taipings would overrun their treaty port. Gordon, a devout evangelical Christian, found himself in the peculiar position of fighting against a Chinese Christian sect on behalf of a Confucian empire. He was good at his job. He would later die at Khartoum fighting another self-proclaimed messiah, the Mahdi, which tells you something about the century.

## The End

By 1864 Nanjing was surrounded. Inside the Heavenly Capital, Hong Xiuquan was fifty years old, increasingly unhinged, eating "manna" — which was to say, weeds from the palace garden — and telling his followers God would provide. In June, he died. The official cause was illness. It may have been suicide by poison. The weeds may simply have killed him.

In July, Zeng Guofan's troops breached the walls. They sacked the city for three days. Hong's fifteen-year-old son was captured and executed by slow slicing, the traditional punishment for rebellion against the throne. The Heavenly Kingdom of Great Peace was finished.

## What's Left

The Qing dynasty won, but it never really recovered. The military power that had been concentrated in Manchu banner forces was now permanently devolved to regional Han armies — Zeng Guofan's Hunan Army, Li Hongzhang's Anhui Army. Fifty years later, when the dynasty finally fell in 1911, it would fall partly because those regional warlords were stronger than the throne.

Mao Zedong, as a young man in the 1920s, studied the Taiping Rebellion obsessively. He took lessons from it: the importance of peasant mobilization, the power of an ideology that promised land redistribution, the way a determined minority could overrun a corrupt empire. He also took lessons from its failures — the infighting, the detachment of the leadership from its base, the way charismatic authority curdled into paranoia. A century after Hong's death, Mao's peasant armies would do what Hong's never quite managed.

What stays with you, though, is not the geopolitics. It is the arithmetic. Twenty million people, give or take ten million — the error bars alone contain every person killed in the First World War — died because a village schoolteacher failed an exam four times, read a pamphlet, and decided the solution to his cosmic humiliation was to become the brother of God.

It is worth asking, once in a while, how many of the defining catastrophes of history began in a moment that small.
