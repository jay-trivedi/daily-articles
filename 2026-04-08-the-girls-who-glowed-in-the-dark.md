# The Girls Who Glowed in the Dark

*April 8, 2026*

---

In 1920, a young woman named Grace Fryer left her job at the United States Radium Corporation in Orange, New Jersey. She felt fine. She was twenty-three years old, healthy, and had spent three years painting luminous watch dials for a living — a good job, a modern job, the kind of job a girl in the 1910s was lucky to get.

By 1924, her jawbone looked like Swiss cheese.

This is the story of the Radium Girls — the approximately 4,000 young women who painted glowing dials in American factories between 1917 and 1929, the company executives who knew they were poisoning them, and how their suffering quietly rewrote the rules of American labor.

---

## Lip, Dip, Paint

The work itself was elegant. Factories produced watch faces, clock dials, military instrument panels — all requiring numerals that glowed in the dark. The paint that made them glow was mixed with radium. To apply it precisely, painters needed a fine, sharp brush tip. Supervisors taught every new worker the same technique: press the bristles between your lips, dip the brush into the paint, apply to dial. Repeat.

Lip, dip, paint. All day, every day.

The girls swallowed trace amounts of radium with every lick. And because radium, chemically, behaves almost identically to calcium — the body treated it as such. It wasn't absorbed and flushed out. It was incorporated directly into their bones, embedded in the tissue that lines the skeleton. Then it stayed there and irradiated them from the inside, for the rest of their lives.

Nobody told them any of this. Some supervisors swallowed the paint themselves to demonstrate its harmlessness. Painters went home with glowing hair, glowing skin, glowing clothes. At night, they glowed in the dark. They thought it was delightful. They painted their nails, their faces, their teeth with the stuff as a joke. They were nineteen, twenty, twenty-one years old.

Meanwhile, company scientists worked behind lead screens. They wore protective gloves. They used tongs to handle samples. They knew.

---

## The First Signs

The first woman to die was Amelia "Mollie" Maggia, in September 1922. It started with a toothache. Then another tooth. Then the extractions began, and after each one, the wound wouldn't heal. Her mouth filled with painful sores. Her jawbone had to be removed. Doctors, unable to explain what was happening to her, diagnosed syphilis — a convenient, stigmatizing misdiagnosis that allowed the company to deny any connection to its product.

Mollie Maggia was twenty-four when she died.

She wasn't alone. Women across the factory were developing the same constellation of symptoms: crumbling teeth, jawbones that deteriorated to nothing, severe anemia, collapsed vertebrae. Some women needed neck-to-waist braces to hold themselves upright. Others developed tumors that moved through their bodies like slow fire.

The company, US Radium Corporation, had its own medical researchers. In 1924, it commissioned a study of worker health from respected Harvard physicians Cecil and Katherine Drinker. The Drinkers found exactly what you'd expect: heavy contamination, blood conditions that couldn't be explained any other way, workers showing unmistakable signs of radiation poisoning. Their report said so, clearly.

Arthur Roeder, the company's president, rewrote it before submitting it to the New Jersey Department of Labor. The altered version concluded that every girl was "in perfect condition."

It was Harvard public health researcher Alice Hamilton who uncovered the forgery. She helped ensure the true findings were published — through the *New York World* newspaper, not quietly through scientific channels — and the fraud became public.

---

## The Lawsuit

Grace Fryer spent two years trying to find a lawyer willing to take her case. Law firms were reluctant to take on US Radium Corporation. When she finally filed suit in May 1927, she was thirty years old and couldn't raise her arms high enough to take an oath in court. She was joined by four other women. Together they became known as the Radium Girls.

They didn't have long. Radium poisoning is slow but thorough, and all five plaintiffs were visibly, measurably dying. The company's legal strategy was simple: delay. Motion after motion. Postponement after postponement. If they could drag the case out long enough, there would be no plaintiffs left to compensate.

The judge was unmoved. The case moved forward.

On October 16, 1927, attorneys had Mollie Maggia's body exhumed — five years after her death. An autopsy was performed. Her bones lit up a Geiger counter from across the room. They were intensely, unmistakably radioactive. The syphilis diagnosis was exposed as fiction. The company had killed her and hidden it.

On June 4, 1928, US Radium Corporation settled. Each of the five women received $10,000 (about $188,000 today) plus $600 annually for the remainder of their lives. Grace Fryer died in 1933, at thirty-four. The others followed.

In Illinois, fifteen more women fought a separate lawsuit against the Radium Dial Company in Ottawa — a case so contentious that the company relocated to avoid jurisdiction. Catherine Donohue, who led the Illinois group, was so ill by the time the case concluded in 1938 that she had to testify from a cot. She died the following year, at thirty-five. Her case forced Illinois to amend its Occupational Diseases Act to include radiation poisoning as a compensable workplace injury.

---

## What They Left Behind

The Radium Girls didn't survive to see most of what they built.

Their cases established, for the first time, a legal precedent that workers could sue employers for damages resulting from occupational hazards — and win. It wasn't obvious that they could, before 1928.

In 1949, Congress passed legislation extending compensation to all workers with occupational diseases. In 1970, Congress created the Occupational Safety and Health Administration. The congressional record cited the Radium Girls.

Radium-based paint was banned in the 1960s. Maximum permissible doses for radiation workers — the standards nuclear power plants, hospitals, and research labs use today — were derived in part from the doses reconstructed from the Radium Girls' exposures. Their bones became data. Their deaths were, eventually, calibration points.

The graves of some Radium Girls still register above background radiation levels. You can detect them with the right equipment, a century later.

There's something worth sitting with in that. These women were not scientists or politicians or industry leaders. They had a good job at a factory. They did what their supervisors told them. They glowed in the dark and thought it was fun. And when their bodies started falling apart and the company denied everything, they found lawyers, sat in courtrooms, testified from cots, and refused to be made into a footnote.

The American worker's right to a safe workplace was not handed down by enlightened industrialists. It was extracted, painfully, by women who were already dying.
