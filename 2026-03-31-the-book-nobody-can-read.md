# The Book Nobody Can Read

Somewhere inside the Beinecke Rare Book & Manuscript Library at Yale University, behind climate-controlled glass, sits a 600-year-old book that has made fools of every brilliant mind that has ever tried to read it.

It has 240 pages of elegant handwriting in a script that matches no known alphabet. It contains detailed illustrations of plants that don't exist, star charts that don't correspond to any known astronomical system, and dozens of small naked women bathing in networks of interconnected pipes and green liquid. Nobody knows who wrote it, what language it's in, or what it's about.

It's called the Voynich Manuscript, and it is — without exaggeration — the most mysterious document in human history.

## The Book Dealer and the Jesuits

In 1912, a Polish-Lithuanian book dealer named Wilfrid Voynich was rummaging through a collection of old manuscripts at the Villa Mondragone, a Jesuit college near Frascati, just outside Rome. The Jesuits were quietly selling off parts of their library to raise money. Among the dusty piles, Voynich found something that stopped him cold.

The manuscript was small — roughly the size of a modern paperback, but thick. Its pages were made of vellum — calfskin — and covered in a flowing, looping script that looked almost familiar but resolved into nothing recognizable. Every page was different. Some showed elaborate botanical drawings of plants with impossibly bulbous roots and leaves that curled in ways no real plant does. Others showed circular diagrams filled with stars, zodiac symbols, and radiating text. And then there were the pages with the women — small, nude female figures standing in pools, floating in tubes, clustered around what looked like plumbing from some medieval spa.

Voynich was electrified. Tucked inside the manuscript, he found a letter dated 1665 from Johannes Marcus Marci, a physician in Prague, sending the book to the Jesuit scholar Athanasius Kircher. The letter mentioned that the manuscript had once belonged to Emperor Rudolf II of the Holy Roman Empire, who had purchased it for 600 gold ducats — somewhere around $90,000 in today's money. Rudolf believed it was the work of the English friar Roger Bacon, the 13th-century polymath who wrote about optics, alchemy, and the secrets of nature.

If that provenance was correct, someone had been trying to figure out this book for at least 250 years before Voynich ever touched it.

## What's Actually In It

Scholars have divided the manuscript into six rough sections based on the illustrations, since — obviously — they can't read the text.

**The Herbal Section** takes up nearly half the book. It contains 113 drawings of plants, each accompanied by blocks of text. Some of the plants look vaguely realistic — you can almost convince yourself one might be a sunflower or a fern. But most are botanical chimeras: roots from one species grafted onto leaves from another, stems that split into impossible geometries. No botanist has ever definitively identified a single one.

**The Astronomical Section** features circular diagrams that look like star charts or cosmological maps. Some pages show zodiac symbols — Pisces, Taurus, Sagittarius — surrounded by concentric rings of 30 small female figures, each holding a star. Strangely, the zodiac sequence begins with Pisces rather than Aries, which is unusual for any known European or Islamic tradition.

**The Balneological Section** — and this is where things get truly weird — shows networks of interconnected tubes and pools filled with those small naked women. Some appear to be bathing. Others seem to float through organic-looking pipework that resembles intestines or blood vessels. The purpose of these illustrations is anyone's guess. Recent scholars have suggested they might depict gynecological or reproductive knowledge, but that's one theory among dozens.

**The Pharmaceutical Section** shows drawings of what appear to be jars and containers alongside plant parts, suggesting recipes or remedies. The final section contains dense pages of text with star-like markers in the margins, resembling some kind of index or recipe collection.

All of it is written in the same confident, flowing script. Whoever wrote this wasn't hesitating. They knew exactly what they were putting on the page.

## The Codebreakers Who Failed

Here's what makes the Voynich Manuscript genuinely extraordinary, rather than just quirky: the caliber of people who have tried — and failed — to crack it.

William Friedman is arguably the greatest cryptanalyst in American history. During World War II, he led the team that broke Japan's PURPLE cipher, one of the most complex encryption systems ever devised. Breaking PURPLE helped win the war in the Pacific. Friedman was, without overstatement, the man who could read anything.

He spent thirty years on the Voynich Manuscript.

In the 1940s, Friedman assembled the "First Study Group" — a team of top-tier government cryptographers who met after hours to work on the manuscript. They transcribed every line of text onto IBM punch cards, making it one of the earliest documents subjected to computational analysis. In 1962, he formed a second study group that attempted to feed the text into an RCA computer. Neither effort produced a solution.

Before his death in 1969, Friedman deposited his conclusion in a sealed envelope at the National Security Agency. When it was opened, it read: the manuscript was written in "an early attempt to construct an artificial or universal language of the a priori type." In other words, Friedman didn't think it was a code at all. He believed someone had invented an entirely new language from scratch — a synthetic tongue with its own grammar and vocabulary, built from logical categories rather than evolved naturally. If true, it would predate the known history of constructed languages by centuries.

John Tiltman, a legendary British codebreaker who cracked multiple German and Japanese ciphers in World War II, also spent years on the manuscript. He couldn't crack it either, but he made one crucial observation: the text had internal structure. Words followed patterns. Certain characters appeared only at the beginnings of words, others only at the ends. Whatever this was, it wasn't random gibberish. It behaved like a real language.

## The Statistical Paradox

And that's the heart of the mystery. The Voynich text doesn't act like a hoax, but it doesn't act quite like a natural language either.

Statistical analysis shows that the text follows Zipf's Law — a mathematical pattern where the most common word in a text appears roughly twice as often as the second most common word, three times as often as the third, and so on. Every natural language on Earth follows Zipf's Law. So does the Voynich text. Random nonsense does not.

But there are anomalies. The text has almost no single-letter words and very few two-letter words, which is unusual for most known languages. Words repeat far more often than you'd expect — sometimes the same word appears two or three times in a row. The entropy (a measure of information density) is lower than most European languages but higher than meaningless noise.

It's as if the text is hovering in some uncanny valley between language and non-language — structured enough to be meaningful, but strange enough to defy every framework we have.

## The Modern Hunt

In 2009, researchers at the University of Arizona carbon-dated the vellum to between 1404 and 1438, firmly placing the physical material in early 15th-century Europe. But there's a catch critics love to point out: you can date the skin, not the ink. It was historically common for scribes — or forgers — to write on older blank parchment.

Still, the manuscript keeps yielding secrets to modern technology. In September 2024, multispectral imaging scans revealed details invisible to the naked eye — underlayers and retouching in the plant illustrations that align with early 15th-century European artistic techniques. That same year, researchers Keegan Brewer and Michelle Lewis published a study arguing that the manuscript is essentially a medieval women's health manual, focused on conception, pregnancy, and gynecology. The bathing nymphs? Therapeutic baths. The plants? Herbal remedies for reproductive health. The tubes? Anatomical diagrams drawn before anyone had a clear picture of human anatomy.

Then in 2025, a study proposed a historically plausible cipher called "Naibbe" — a verbose substitution system that could encode Latin and Italian text, was achievable with 15th-century materials, and reproduces many of the manuscript's known statistical properties. It's the most credible cipher theory yet, though it remains unverified.

## Why It Still Matters

Six hundred years. Emperors, Jesuits, wartime codebreakers, supercomputers, artificial intelligence — and still, nobody can read this book.

The Voynich Manuscript matters not because of what it might say (though that would certainly be interesting) but because of what it represents: a hard limit on human cleverness. We live in an age where we've sequenced the human genome, detected gravitational waves from colliding black holes, and photographed the event horizon of a supermassive black hole. We have cracked every cipher ever used in warfare. We have translated dead languages from civilizations that vanished millennia ago.

And we cannot read a 240-page book with pictures.

Maybe it's a medieval medical text. Maybe it's an alchemist's personal notebook in a private script. Maybe it's a cunning hoax by a 15th-century genius who understood information theory four centuries before Claude Shannon was born. Or maybe — and this is the possibility that keeps people up at night — it's something we haven't thought of yet. A category of human knowledge production that doesn't fit into any box we currently have.

The book sits in its case at Yale, open to a page of impossible plants, waiting patiently for someone smart enough to hear what it's been trying to say for six centuries. So far, it's still waiting.
