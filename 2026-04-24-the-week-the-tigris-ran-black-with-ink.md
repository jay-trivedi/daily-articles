# The Week the Tigris Ran Black with Ink

In February 1258, the greatest city on earth died in seven days.

Baghdad had stood for 500 years as the beating heart of Islamic civilization — a metropolis of perhaps a million souls, the richest city west of China, the place where Greek philosophy had been rescued, Indian numerals translated, algebra invented, and the first truly universal library in human history assembled. By the time the Mongol army under Hulagu Khan finished with it, contemporaries said two things with morbid consistency: the Tigris ran red with blood for miles downstream, and it ran black with ink from the books thrown into its current.

Both things were probably true.

## The city that thought it was eternal

The Abbasid Caliphate had ruled from Baghdad since 762 CE, when the second Abbasid caliph, al-Mansur, founded a perfectly circular city on the west bank of the Tigris and called it Madinat al-Salam — "the City of Peace." By the ninth century, under Harun al-Rashid and his son al-Ma'mun, Baghdad had become the intellectual capital of the world. Al-Ma'mun's father had dreamed of Aristotle, so al-Ma'mun had paid translators their weight in gold to render Greek science into Arabic. The result was the Bayt al-Hikma — the House of Wisdom — where scholars translated, annotated, and argued over every surviving work of Euclid, Ptolemy, Galen, Plato, and Aristotle, along with Sanskrit astronomy and Persian medicine.

It was in Baghdad that al-Khwarizmi wrote the book whose title gave the world the word *algebra*. It was in Baghdad that Arabic numerals (themselves Indian) were standardized and given to the West. It was in Baghdad that hospitals with separate wards, licensed physicians, and written prescriptions became routine 500 years before Europe caught up. By 1258 the political power of the Abbasid caliphs had long since faded — they had become ceremonial figureheads presiding over sultans and emirs — but the city itself remained. It was unthinkable that Baghdad could simply end.

## The grandson of Genghis

Hulagu Khan was the grandson of Genghis Khan and the brother of Kublai, the future emperor of China. His other brother, Möngke, was the Great Khan of the Mongol Empire, and Möngke had given Hulagu a job: ride west, crush the Assassins, and bring the Abbasid caliph to heel. The Mongol empire ran on a single principle — submit or die — and the caliph, al-Musta'sim, had not submitted.

Al-Musta'sim was the 37th Abbasid caliph, and by most accounts a weak one. When Hulagu's envoys arrived in 1257 demanding that he dismantle Baghdad's defenses and pay tribute, al-Musta'sim refused, insisting that any attack on the Commander of the Faithful would cause the Muslim world to rise against the Mongols. This was wishful thinking. His vizier had already written secret letters of goodwill to Hulagu. His generals had not been paid. His walls had not been maintained.

Hulagu arrived outside Baghdad in January 1258 with an army that modern estimates put at 120,000 to 150,000 men — possibly the largest force the Mongols had ever fielded. It included Chinese siege engineers with gunpowder bombs and enormous catapults. Armenian and Georgian Christians rode in his cavalry. The city's defensive garrison numbered perhaps 50,000, most of them untested.

On February 5th, the walls were breached. On February 10th, the caliph surrendered. On February 13th, the slaughter began.

## Seven days

For a week, the Mongols did what they had done to Samarkand, to Bukhara, to Herat, to Merv — they killed systematically. The low estimate of the dead is 90,000; the high estimate exceeds a million; most modern historians land somewhere between 200,000 and 800,000. The stench of decomposing bodies grew so intolerable that Hulagu himself moved his camp upwind.

The House of Wisdom was burned. Every mosque of consequence was burned. The great hospitals were burned. Libraries that had taken four centuries to assemble were emptied into the Tigris. A Persian historian writing a generation later claimed that a horseman could cross the river on a bridge of books, and that the water ran black with dissolved ink for days. Even accounting for medieval hyperbole, the core of the story holds: an irreplaceable fraction of the human record — originals of lost Greek texts, unique Arabic commentaries, astronomical tables, medical treatises, mathematical proofs — simply ceased to exist.

On February 20th, al-Musta'sim was brought out of hiding. The Mongols had a strict taboo against spilling royal blood on the earth, which they considered sacred. So al-Musta'sim was wrapped tightly in one of his own carpets and a line of Mongol cavalry rode over him until he stopped moving. His sons were killed the same day. One daughter was taken as a concubine; one young son was sent to Mongolia, where he vanishes from the record.

The 500-year Abbasid caliphate ended with a man suffocating in a rug.

## What was actually lost

It is tempting to romanticize the House of Wisdom as a single burning library, a kind of Islamic Alexandria. The reality is messier and in some ways worse. The House of Wisdom as a formal institution had peaked four centuries earlier; by 1258, Baghdad's intellectual life was distributed across dozens of private libraries, madrasas, and court collections. The Mongols did not destroy one library. They destroyed all of them, and the networks of patronage, teaching, and copying that had produced them.

Before 1258, the center of gravity of world science was unambiguously in the Muslim world — in Baghdad, Cairo, Córdoba, Samarkand. After 1258, it never was again. Islamic scholarship did not die; Timurid astronomy in Samarkand and Ottoman medicine in Istanbul would still produce extraordinary work. But the thick, continuous, argumentative intellectual culture that had linked Kufa to Córdoba for 500 years was broken, and it did not re-form.

Baghdad itself became a provincial town. Its population in 1300 may have been under 150,000. It would be ruled by Mongols, then Turkmens, then Safavids, then Ottomans, then the British. It would not be capital of anything of consequence again until 1921, when the British installed a Hashemite king over a new country called Iraq.

## The cost of one decision

There is a small, haunting detail buried in the sources. In the months before the siege, al-Musta'sim's treasury was full. When Hulagu demanded tribute, the caliph could have paid. When the Mongols asked for the walls to be dismantled as a gesture of submission, he could have dismantled them. When his vizier urged him to flee the city with what scholars and books he could carry, he refused, because he believed that God would not allow the city of the Prophet's successor to fall.

Hulagu, after taking the city, is said to have locked al-Musta'sim in a room with his own treasure and asked why, if he had so much gold, he had not used it to buy soldiers or tribute. The caliph had no answer.

The books in the Tigris did not have an answer either. They simply dissolved, one ink stroke at a time, into a river that had been running past that city since before writing existed — and would keep running, indifferent, long after the last witness to what had been lost was also gone.
