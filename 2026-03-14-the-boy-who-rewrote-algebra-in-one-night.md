# The Boy Who Rewrote Algebra in One Night

On the evening of May 29, 1832, a twenty-year-old Frenchman sat alone at a desk in Paris, scribbling furiously by candlelight. He knew he would almost certainly die the next morning. He had a pistol duel at dawn, and he was not a good shot. So Évariste Galois did the only thing that made sense to him — he tried to cram the entirety of his mathematical vision onto paper before the sun came up.

In the margins of his frantic notes, he scrawled: *"I have not time; I have not time."*

Those seven pages, dashed off in a single desperate night, would go on to reshape the foundations of modern algebra. Mathematician Hermann Weyl later called this letter "perhaps the most substantial piece of writing in the whole literature of mankind."

This is the story of how a teenager solved a 350-year-old problem, was ignored by every institution that should have celebrated him, and died before his twenty-first birthday — leaving behind ideas so far ahead of their time that it took other mathematicians over a decade just to understand what he had written.

---

## A Bright Kid in a Dark Time

Évariste Galois was born on October 25, 1811, in Bourg-la-Reine, a small town just south of Paris. His father, Nicolas-Gabriel Galois, was the town's mayor — a witty, well-liked man with strong Republican sympathies. His mother, Adélaïde-Marie, was well-educated and gave young Évariste his early schooling at home until he was twelve.

When Galois entered the Lycée Louis-le-Grand in Paris at age twelve, he was initially a good student. But by fifteen, something shifted. He got his hands on a copy of Adrien-Marie Legendre's *Éléments de Géométrie* — a serious textbook meant for much older students — and reportedly read it "like a novel," mastering it on the first pass. Soon after, he was devouring the original research papers of Joseph-Louis Lagrange, work intended for professional mathematicians, not schoolboys.

His regular classes bored him to tears. His teachers described him as "singular" and "original" but also "closed" and difficult. He had found mathematics, and nothing else would ever matter as much.

## The Problem That Had Stumped Everyone

Here's what Galois was obsessing over. Since the Renaissance, mathematicians had known formulas for solving polynomial equations up to degree four. You probably remember the quadratic formula from school — that handles degree two. Italian mathematicians like Cardano and Ferrari had cracked degrees three and four in the 1500s.

But degree five? Nobody could find a formula. For 350 years, the best mathematical minds in Europe had tried and failed. In 1824, the Norwegian mathematician Niels Henrik Abel proved that no *general* formula could exist for the quintic. But Abel's result left a tantalizing question hanging: even if there's no universal formula, *which specific* fifth-degree equations can be solved by radicals (using only addition, subtraction, multiplication, division, and root extraction), and which can't?

Galois, still a teenager, answered this question completely. Not just for degree five, but for *every* degree. His insight was breathtaking in its abstraction: he showed that whether an equation is solvable depends on the internal symmetry structure of its roots. He invented an entirely new mathematical object — what we now call a *group* — to capture these symmetries, and proved that an equation is solvable by radicals if and only if its associated group has a specific structural property (being "solvable," a term that still bears the imprint of this origin).

He had, almost single-handedly, invented group theory — one of the most important branches of modern mathematics.

## The World Refused to Listen

You might expect that a teenager solving a centuries-old problem would be met with acclaim. Instead, Galois ran headfirst into a wall of institutional indifference and bad luck that reads like a dark comedy.

In 1829, at age seventeen, he submitted his first paper to the French Academy of Sciences. It was received by Augustin-Louis Cauchy, one of the most prominent mathematicians alive. Cauchy lost it. Just... lost it.

That same year, Galois's father killed himself. The local priest in Bourg-la-Reine had forged the mayor's name on cruel, mocking poems aimed at his own family. Nicolas-Gabriel Galois, a gentle and well-liked man, could not bear the scandal. On July 2, 1829, he hanged himself in his Paris apartment, just steps from the school where his son was studying.

Weeks after his father's suicide, a devastated Galois sat for the entrance exam to the École Polytechnique, France's most prestigious school for mathematics. He failed. The story goes that when an examiner asked a question Galois found stupid, he threw an eraser at the man's head. Whether or not the eraser detail is apocryphal, the failure was real, and it stung.

In 1830, Galois submitted a revised memoir to the Academy. This time it was entrusted to Joseph Fourier, who was supposed to review it. Fourier died. The manuscript vanished with him.

In 1831, Galois tried a third time, submitting to the Academy yet again. This time, Siméon-Denis Poisson actually read it — and rejected it. Poisson said the work was "incomprehensible." He was not being cruel; he genuinely could not follow Galois's reasoning. The ideas were simply too far ahead of anything that existed.

Three submissions. One lost, one dead-ended with a dead reviewer, one rejected. Galois was twenty years old and had been rebuffed by every mathematical institution in France.

## Revolution, Prison, and a Mysterious Woman

Mathematics wasn't Galois's only passion. He was a fervent Republican during one of the most turbulent periods in French history. After the July Revolution of 1830, he threw himself into radical politics with the same intensity he brought to algebra. He was arrested twice — once for allegedly threatening King Louis-Philippe at a banquet (he may have raised a glass with a knife in hand, which was interpreted as a threat), and once for wearing the banned uniform of the Artillery of the National Guard.

He spent several months in Sainte-Pélagie prison. When he was released in April 1832, he was twenty years old, politically radicalized, socially isolated, and mathematically frustrated.

Then came a woman. Her name was Stéphanie-Félicie Poterin du Motel. The details are murky. Galois apparently fell for her, and the situation somehow led to a challenge to a duel. Was it a jealous rival? A political setup disguised as a matter of honor? Historians have debated this for nearly two centuries. Some recent scholarship suggests Galois may have even engineered his own death, choosing the duel as a form of political martyrdom. We may never know.

What we do know is that on the night of May 29, 1832, Galois believed he was going to die. So he wrote.

## The Letter

He wrote three letters that night. Two were political, addressed to his Republican comrades. The third — the one that would change mathematics — was to his friend Auguste Chevalier.

In it, Galois laid out the core of his mathematical theory in seven dense, hurried pages. He sketched new theorems, posed conjectures, and begged Chevalier to get the work to Carl Friedrich Gauss and Carl Gustav Jacob Jacobi — the giants of the field — so that someone, someday, would understand what he had seen.

*"Ask Jacobi or Gauss publicly to give their opinion, not as to the truth, but as to the importance of these theorems,"* he wrote. *"Later there will be, I hope, some people who will find it to their advantage to decipher all this mess."*

At dawn on May 30, 1832, Galois faced his opponent. He was shot in the abdomen. Left on the ground, he was eventually found by a passing farmer and taken to a hospital. His younger brother Alfred rushed to his side. According to Alfred, Évariste's last words were: *"Don't cry, Alfred. I need all my courage to die at twenty."*

He died the next morning, May 31, 1832.

## The Resurrection

Galois's manuscripts sat neglected for over a decade. Then, in 1843, Joseph Liouville finally sat down and worked through the papers carefully. He was stunned. In 1846, he published them in his *Journal de Mathématiques Pures et Appliquées*, announcing to the mathematical world that Galois had been right all along.

The ideas exploded outward. Group theory became one of the central pillars of abstract algebra, and today it underpins everything from quantum physics to cryptography, from the classification of crystal structures to the error-correcting codes in your phone. Galois theory — the specific framework he invented — remains a cornerstone of modern mathematics, taught in every serious algebra course worldwide.

---

Here's the thing that stays with me about Galois. He wasn't just ahead of his time — he was operating in a mathematical language that *didn't exist yet*, one that he was inventing as he went. The people who rejected him weren't fools; they were accomplished mathematicians who simply couldn't see what he saw. It took the rest of the field fourteen years to catch up to a teenager.

And on his last night alive, with a bullet waiting for him at sunrise, he chose to spend those final hours not writing goodbye letters or making peace with God, but trying to explain mathematics. Because to Galois, mathematics *was* the important thing — the thing worth leaving behind, the thing that might outlast a short, furious, brilliant life.

He was right about that, too.
