# The Farm Boy Who Invented Television — And Was Erased From History

In 1922, a 14-year-old boy named Philo Farnsworth was plowing a potato field in Rigby, Idaho, when he looked back at the neat parallel rows his plow had carved into the earth. And in that moment — staring at furrows of dirt — he figured out how to make television work.

Not television as it existed at the time, which was a clunky, mechanical contraption that used spinning disks with holes punched in them to scan images line by line. No. Farnsworth saw something entirely different in those rows: what if you could scan an image the same way you plow a field? Back and forth, row by row, but using a beam of electrons instead of a blade? No moving parts. Pure electricity. An idea so ahead of its time that the world's largest electronics corporation would spend over a decade trying to steal it from him.

## The Kid Who Wouldn't Shut Up About Electrons

Philo Taylor Farnsworth was born on August 19, 1906, in a log cabin in southwestern Utah — a cabin his grandfather had built as a follower of Brigham Young. The family had no electricity, no telephone. When they moved to a farm in Idaho in 1918, 12-year-old Philo discovered a stack of old technology magazines in the attic and devoured them. He rigged the family's hand-cranked washing machine to run on electricity. He was, by every account, a relentless tinkerer.

But the potato field moment was different. This wasn't tinkering — it was a conceptual breakthrough. Back at Rigby High School, Farnsworth cornered his chemistry teacher, Justin Tolman, and sketched his idea on the blackboard: a vacuum tube that could dissect an image into lines of electrons, transmit them, and reassemble them on a screen. Tolman was stunned. He kept those blackboard sketches, and years later, they would save Farnsworth's life's work.

## 202 Green Street

Farnsworth's father died in 1924, forcing him to drop out of Brigham Young University to support his family. He bounced between odd jobs — railroad worker, radio installer — but the television idea never left him. In 1926, at age 19, he convinced two businessmen from Salt Lake City, George Everson and Leslie Gorrell, to put up $6,000 to fund his research. He promised them he could build a working electronic television.

They set up a tiny lab at 202 Green Street in San Francisco. Farnsworth, now married to his high school sweetheart Pem Gardner (who served as his lab assistant), worked feverishly. He was 20 years old, had no degree, and was trying to solve a problem that some of the best-funded labs in the world hadn't cracked.

On September 7, 1927, it happened. Farnsworth's "image dissector" — the camera tube he'd conceived in that potato field — transmitted its first image to a receiver in another room: a single straight line. When his brother-in-law rotated the glass slide, the line on the receiver rotated too. It worked.

"There you are," Farnsworth said quietly. "Electronic television."

He was 21 years old.

Over the next weeks, the lab transmitted increasingly complex images — triangles, squares, and, in a nod to his investors, a dollar sign. When Everson telegrammed the news to the backers, legend has it the dollar sign was the image that got them excited.

## Enter the Shark

David Sarnoff was not a scientist. He was something more dangerous: a visionary businessman. As president of the Radio Corporation of America — RCA — Sarnoff had turned radio into a household necessity and built a media empire. Now he wanted television, and he wanted RCA to own it completely.

Sarnoff had his own television man: Vladimir Zworykin, a brilliant Russian-born engineer who had filed a patent for an electronic TV camera tube called the "Iconoscope" back in 1923. There was one problem — Zworykin's 1923 design didn't actually work. He wouldn't produce a functioning version until 1931.

In 1930, Sarnoff sent Zworykin to visit Farnsworth's San Francisco lab. It was framed as a collegial visit between fellow inventors. It was anything but. Zworykin studied Farnsworth's image dissector carefully. "This is a beautiful instrument," he reportedly said. "I wish I had invented it."

He went back to RCA and told Sarnoff that Farnsworth's system worked — and that RCA could build its own version for about $100,000. Sarnoff made Farnsworth an offer: sell the whole company to RCA for $100,000. Farnsworth would hand over all his patents and come work for Sarnoff as an employee.

Farnsworth refused. He hadn't sweated through years of poverty and 16-hour lab days to become someone's employee. "There's nothing here we'd need," Sarnoff reportedly told his team after being rejected. It was a lie, and everyone knew it.

## The War

What followed was one of the ugliest patent battles in American history. RCA filed an interference claim, arguing that Zworykin's 1923 patent application had priority over Farnsworth's invention. The case hinged on a critical question: had Zworykin actually built a working device before Farnsworth?

The answer was no. And the proof came from an unlikely source — that chemistry teacher, Justin Tolman. He showed up to the patent hearing and produced, from memory and old notes, the blackboard sketches that 14-year-old Farnsworth had drawn in 1922. They matched the image dissector perfectly.

In 1934, the U.S. Patent Office ruled in Farnsworth's favor. He was the inventor of electronic television. Period.

But RCA didn't stop. Sarnoff launched appeal after appeal, dragging the case through the courts for years. His strategy was brutal and simple: bleed Farnsworth dry. Every month of litigation cost Farnsworth money he didn't have. Every year of delay ate into his 17-year patent window — years during which he should have been licensing his invention and building a business.

It wasn't until 1939 — a full decade after the first patent was granted — that RCA finally agreed to license Farnsworth's patents. It was the first time in RCA's history that the company had paid royalties to an outside inventor. But by then, Farnsworth's patents had only a few years left. And then World War II hit, freezing commercial television development entirely. By the time TV finally exploded into American living rooms in the late 1940s, Farnsworth's patents had expired.

He made almost nothing from the invention that changed the world.

## The Erasure

Sarnoff, meanwhile, spent decades positioning Zworykin as "the father of television." RCA's PR machine was relentless. Magazines, books, TV specials — they all told the same story: television was invented at RCA. Farnsworth slipped into obscurity.

The toll on Farnsworth was devastating. He battled depression, turned to alcohol, and spent time in hospitals. He poured his remaining years into fusion research, inventing the Farnsworth Fusor — a small nuclear fusion device that, while not practical for power generation, is still used today as a neutron source in labs around the world. Even in decline, the man couldn't stop inventing.

There's one moment that cuts through all the tragedy. In 1957, Farnsworth appeared as a mystery guest on the TV game show *I've Got a Secret*. The panel had to guess his secret: "I invented electronic television." They couldn't figure it out. The audience laughed. The man who made the very medium they were watching possible was a stranger to them.

Philo Farnsworth and David Sarnoff both died in 1971. Sarnoff was celebrated as a titan of industry, a broadcasting pioneer. Farnsworth was broke, broken, and forgotten.

## The Coda

In 2000, the state of Utah placed a statue of Farnsworth in the U.S. Capitol's National Statuary Hall — one of only two figures each state is allowed. His plaque reads simply: "Father of Television."

There's something almost unbearably poignant about the Farnsworth story. A teenager looks at rows in a potato field and sees the future. He builds it with his own hands. And then the future is taken from him — not because he was wrong, not because he failed, but because he was one man against a machine. The invention survived. The inventor didn't.

Every time you turn on a screen — any screen — you're looking at Philo Farnsworth's potato field. You just didn't know it.
