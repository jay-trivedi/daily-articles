# The War That Made Hannibal

History remembers Hannibal Barca for the elephants in the Alps, the slaughter at Cannae, the seventeen years he spent burning his way up and down Italy until Rome finally cornered him. We remember him as Rome's greatest enemy. What we forget is that the boy who would become that enemy was nine years old when he watched his father wage a war so cruel that the historian Polybius — a man who had seen plenty — said he could find nothing in the past to compare to it. It had no name at the time. Polybius eventually called it the **Truceless War**. And it nearly destroyed Carthage three years before Hannibal was old enough to swear his famous oath against Rome.

The war is half-forgotten today, sandwiched awkwardly between the First and Second Punic Wars. But it is the keystone. Without it, there is no Hannibal in Italy.

## A bill that came due

The First Punic War ended in 241 BC with Carthage on her knees. Twenty-three years of fighting Rome over Sicily had emptied the treasury, and the peace terms — a colossal indemnity of 3,200 talents, payable over ten years — emptied what was left. Carthage didn't have a citizen army. Like a corporation that contracts out its security, she fought with mercenaries: Iberians, Celts, Ligurians, Balearic slingers, Greek deserters, and tens of thousands of Libyans conscripted from her North African subjects.

When the Sicilian campaign ended, roughly 20,000 of these men were shipped home to Carthage to be paid off and sent on their way.

The Carthaginian senate, looking at the indemnity bill from Rome, decided to negotiate the back wages down. The general Gisco, who had led many of these men in Sicily, warned that this was suicidal. He was overruled. The mercenaries were quartered in the city, and as they waited, their grievances ripened into rage. Eventually the senate, panicking at the sight of armed and angry foreign soldiers in the streets, marched them out to a desert camp at Sicca, two hundred kilometres inland, with a small advance on their pay and a promise of more later.

It was the worst possible move. It removed the Carthaginians' leverage and gave the men time, space, and one another's company.

## Spendius and Mathos

Two figures stepped forward from the camp. **Spendius** was a runaway slave from Campania, a Roman possession; if he were ever sent home, crucifixion was the only thing waiting for him. **Mathos** was a Libyan who had led his people through the war and knew that, of all the mercenaries, the Libyans alone could not simply sail away — they lived under Carthaginian taxation that during the war had doubled, and the rural population was already seething.

These two had every reason to make sure peace did *not* break out. When Gisco arrived in the camp with chests of silver to settle accounts, Spendius and Mathos whipped the soldiers into a frenzy, seized Gisco and 700 of his officers, and took them hostage. Then they sent envoys to the Libyan towns. Within weeks, an army of around 70,000 had assembled — mercenaries plus a vast peasant uprising. They besieged the cities of Utica and Hippacritae and cut the land routes to Carthage itself.

Carthage, the great sea empire, suddenly found that the sea was no use to her. The enemy was already inside the wall.

## The atrocity that closed every door

The first Carthaginian general sent against them, Hanno the Great, was a competent administrator and a bad field commander. He bungled an early engagement, and the rebels grew stronger. Carthage recalled him and gave the joint command to **Hamilcar Barca** — the same Hamilcar who had held out in the Sicilian hills against Rome to the very end of the Punic War, undefeated when his city surrendered around him.

Hamilcar fought brilliantly. With a force of perhaps 10,000 men and a corps of war elephants, he won a sharp victory at the Bagradas River, then maneuvered the rebels into a trap and surrounded them. A Numidian prince named Naravas, impressed by Hamilcar, switched sides with 2,000 cavalry. The mercenaries' coalition began to crack.

Hamilcar offered the captured rebels a choice: take service under him, or walk away and never bear arms against Carthage again. It was a generous, almost Roman, gesture.

Spendius and Mathos understood exactly what Hamilcar was doing. If clemency held, their men would melt back into the Carthaginian fold and they themselves would be hunted down. So they did the only thing that could guarantee the war went on: they made peace impossible.

They brought out Gisco and the 700 prisoners. They cut off their hands. Then they cut off their other extremities — feet, noses, genitals. They broke their legs. They threw what was left into a pit and buried them alive. Then they sent word to Hamilcar that any Carthaginian envoy who came to negotiate would be tortured the same way.

Polybius, writing a century later with access to Carthaginian sources, describes this passage with a kind of moral exhaustion. He says he can find no example in any war he has read about that compares. From this point on, neither side took prisoners. Captured rebels were thrown under the feet of elephants. Captured Carthaginians were crucified or worse. Polybius coined a phrase for it: *aspondos polemos* — the war without truce, without herald, without any of the rules by which civilized peoples agreed to constrain their cruelty.

## The Saw

For three years it ground on. Sardinia's garrison mutinied and joined the revolt, killing every Carthaginian on the island. Utica — Carthage's oldest ally in Africa — defected and massacred its Carthaginian garrison too.

The decisive engagement came in late 238 BC. Hamilcar, by now a veteran of the rebels' habits, lured Spendius's main army into a long, narrow defile in the mountains north of Carthage. The valley was shaped, the soldiers said, like the teeth of a saw — and that became the name of the battle. With his elephants and cavalry blocking the exits, Hamilcar simply sealed the rebels inside and waited.

The trapped army ran out of food. They ate their horses. Then their slaves. Then, at last, their prisoners. When Spendius finally went out under truce to beg for terms, Hamilcar took him hostage and sent the elephants in.

What was left of the army of 40,000 was annihilated. Spendius was crucified within sight of Mathos's last garrison at Tunis. When Mathos finally fell, Carthaginian citizens lined the streets and tore him apart with their hands as he was paraded through the city.

The Truceless War was over.

## Rome's quiet knife

The peace lasted exactly long enough for Carthage to count her dead. While Hamilcar was still mopping up in Africa, Rome did something extraordinary. The Sardinian mercenaries who had revolted had been driven out by the islanders themselves and had appealed to Rome for help. Rome agreed — and then, in 237 BC, announced that it was annexing Sardinia and Corsica from Carthage. When Carthage protested that this violated the peace treaty of 241 BC, Rome answered that any objection would be considered a declaration of war, and demanded an additional 1,200 talents on top.

Carthage paid. She had nothing left to fight with.

Polybius, who otherwise admires Rome, calls this episode shameful — the moment when Rome stopped behaving like a republic and started behaving like an opportunist. To Hamilcar, it was simpler. It was theft from a man who had just spent three years saving his country from ruin, by an enemy who had nothing to do with the saving.

## The boy at the altar

Hamilcar took his veterans, his elephants, and his rage, and went to Spain. The official mission was to develop Carthage's silver mines and rebuild the indemnity payments. The unofficial mission was to build, in Iberia, an army loyal not to Carthage's senate but to the Barcid family — an army that could one day march on Rome.

Before he sailed, according to Polybius, he took his nine-year-old son to a temple and made him place his hand on the altar. The boy was made to swear, as long as he lived, never to be a friend of Rome.

That boy was Hannibal. He kept his oath for forty-six years, until he poisoned himself in a hut on the Black Sea coast rather than be handed over to a Roman magistrate.

## The thought worth holding

The Second Punic War — the elephants over the Alps, the lake of blood at Trasimene, the 50,000 Romans dead in a single afternoon at Cannae — is usually told as the tale of one extraordinary man's grudge. But Hannibal was nine when his father swore him to that altar, and his father was not swearing on the wreckage of the First Punic War. He was swearing on the wreckage of a different war, one Rome did not even fight in — a war that almost destroyed his city while Rome stood by, and at the end of which Rome calmly stole two islands from a wounded neighbor.

Empires keep careful books. Carthage's were finally settled at Zama in 202 BC, and the city itself was burned to bare ground in 146 BC, its fields salted in legend if not in fact. But the entry that started the run on the account was made in 237 BC, when a child placed his small hand on a stone, and his father, still smelling of three years of fire, told him what he must remember.

What gets passed from one generation to the next, in a family or in a country, is rarely the official story. It is the wound nobody wrote down.
